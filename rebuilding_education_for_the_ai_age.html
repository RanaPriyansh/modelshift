1. Executive Verdict

Executive Summary

Modern education is neither a failed relic nor an institution that merely needs more technology. Its core public purposes remain indispensable: literacy, numeracy, knowledge, social development, care, civic formation, safeguarding and credible certification. The central defect is that many systems move students through a fixed programme without reliably ensuring mastery, then reward institutions for visible proxies—attendance, grades, test scores, credentials, rankings and completion—more consistently than for durable knowledge, judgment, agency or transfer.

The practical answer is not “less teaching,” “more projects,” or “more AI.” It is a better sequence: strong foundations; frequent diagnosis and feedback; guided practice; authentic application; increasing autonomy; and bounded AI use that improves learning without replacing cognitive work students still need to perform.

The report distinguishes dissatisfaction from measurable failure, association from causation, and attractive reform language from implementation evidence. Country averages are used to identify patterns, not to declare a single national model universally superior. Effect sizes are included where meaningful, but they are not treated as interchangeable across ages, subjects or outcomes.

The 15 most important conclusions

  1. Foundational learning failure is the first-order problem. In PISA 2022, roughly 31% of students across OECD countries were below baseline proficiency in mathematics, with severe socioeconomic gradients. The World Bank estimated that 70% of 10-year-olds in low- and middle-income countries could not read and understand a simple age-appropriate text after pandemic disruption. Rural India has recently recovered some lost ground, yet ASER 2024 still found that only 23.4% of Grade III government-school pupils could read a Grade II text and 27.6% could perform subtraction. These are not mainly “creativity” problems; they are failures to secure the knowledge on which later judgment and creativity depend. High confidence 1 4 5

  2. The “factory-worker design” story is historically overdrawn. Industrialization and bureaucratic scale influenced timetables, age grading, standardization and efficiency. But mass schooling also grew from religious instruction, literacy campaigns, nation-building, democratic citizenship, military and administrative state capacity, child protection and social integration. There was no single original blueprint to manufacture compliant factory labour. High confidence 19 20 21 22

  3. Proxy optimization is real, but measurement is not the enemy. Tests, grades and credentials help allocate scarce opportunities, protect against patronage and reveal system failures. Distortion occurs when high stakes reward test-specific gains, strategic classification, grade inflation or credential accumulation rather than broad competence. The remedy is better assessment architecture—multiple measures, secure core knowledge, authentic performance, oral defence and longitudinal evidence—not the abolition of standards. High confidence 24 25 69

  4. Knowledge and agency are complements, not rivals. Beginners learn efficiently through explicit explanation, worked examples and guided practice because working memory is limited and novices lack organized schemas. As knowledge grows, inquiry, projects, debate, independent research and real-world work become more productive. “Direct instruction versus discovery” is therefore mainly a question of sequencing, guidance and subject matter. High confidence 32 33 34 35

  5. Several low-cost learning practices are more reliable than fashionable whole-school philosophies. Retrieval practice, spacing, feedback, formative assessment, worked examples and metacognitive prompts improve retention or achievement across many settings when implemented well. Their effects are not automatic or equally large in every subject, but their evidence base is stronger than learning-styles matching, generic growth-mindset assemblies or “brain-based” products. High confidence 27 28 29 31 37 38 42 51 52

  6. Teacher capacity is the binding implementation constraint. Teachers cannot personalize, give high-quality feedback, run authentic projects, monitor wellbeing and learn new AI tools while administrative load and fragmented initiatives consume their time. Coaching can improve instruction and achievement, but effects shrink at scale when programmes lose intensity, coherence or protected time. Reform plans that ignore workload are not serious plans. High confidence 10 41 79

  7. High-dosage tutoring and instruction targeted to actual learning level are among the strongest catch-up tools. Experimental tutoring evidence averages about 0.37 standard deviations, while Teaching at the Right Level has produced substantial gains in several Indian implementations. Failed scale-ups show the decisive role of protected time, simple materials, monitoring and implementation support. Training teachers once is not an intervention. High confidence 40 54 55

  8. Class-size reduction is useful in some contexts but is not the universal first purchase. Tennessee’s STAR experiment found benefits from substantially smaller early-grade classes, especially for disadvantaged children, but system-wide reductions are costly and later evidence is heterogeneous. In many budgets, coherent curriculum, tutoring, teacher coaching and attendance support will generate more learning per dollar. Moderate confidence 56 55

  9. Opting out is a heterogeneous response to fit, safety, ideology, cost and institutional trust—not a single movement. Families homeschool because of school environment, bullying, special needs, mental health, religious or philosophical preferences, flexibility and academic concerns. College non-participation reflects cost and uncertain returns as well as attractive work pathways. Alternative settings can improve fit, but outcomes are heavily selected by parental capacity, resources, regulation and student characteristics. High confidence on motives; low on causal superiority 15 16 17 18 85

  10. College remains valuable on average, while the universal-degree default is obsolete. Across OECD countries, tertiary-educated young adults have higher employment and earnings on average, but completion, field, institution, debt and local labour markets create large variation. Degrees also signal persistence and prior ability, not only acquired skill. The future should be more plural: degrees, apprenticeships, licensure, work-integrated programmes and independently verified modular credentials. High confidence 13 24 68 70

  11. AI can raise completed-task performance while weakening learning. A controlled high-school mathematics study found that unrestricted GPT assistance improved practice performance but reduced subsequent unaided exam performance; a guardrailed tutor avoided that harm but did not create a positive exam effect. Other trials show gains when AI scaffolds tutors or requires active explanation. AI access is not a pedagogy: design, timing and assessment determine whether it augments or replaces thought. Moderate confidence; evidence is recent and tool-specific 59 60

  12. AI increases—not decreases—the value of domain knowledge and human responsibility. Generative AI is most exposed to clerical, routine-information and codifiable digital tasks. Jobs are more likely to be transformed than eliminated wholesale, while complementary capabilities—problem selection, judgment, verification, communication, leadership, physical-world context and accountability—remain important. Students need enough knowledge to detect plausible nonsense and enough agency to decide what problems deserve solving. Moderate confidence 57 62 63 64

  13. No country is a copy-and-paste model. Estonia, Singapore, Japan, Korea, Canada and Switzerland perform strongly through different institutional combinations. Finland has declined from its earlier peak. Germany and Switzerland demonstrate the value of respected work-based pathways, while early tracking can reproduce inequality. Culture, migration, family resources, teacher labour markets, curriculum and assessment interact; “copy Finland” is not an implementation strategy. High confidence 1 2 65 66

  14. The smallest coherent reform package is more valuable than a long wish list. A school can make meaningful progress by combining a clear knowledge curriculum, short diagnostic assessments, mastery and reassessment, retrieval and spacing, targeted tutoring, protected teacher collaboration, one authentic application block, secure AI-aware assessment and an attendance/wellbeing response system. Each element supports the others. High confidence on components; moderate on package interaction

  15. The deeper paradigm shift is from time-served credentialing to demonstrated, cumulative capability—without abandoning common institutions. Age cohorts, fixed courses and degrees will remain because they solve social and administrative coordination problems. Systems should nevertheless allow more flexible pacing, multiple pathways and portable evidence of competence while preserving shared civic knowledge, peer community, safeguarding and access to qualified adults. Speculative but strategically important

2. What Education Is Actually For

A first-principles model: capability, membership, care and coordination

Education is not one production function. It is a bundle of institutions that perform four classes of work, and those functions can conflict.

2.1 Capability formation

The first function is to build intellectual, practical, physical and social capabilities that people do not reliably acquire by exposure alone: language, literacy, numeracy, scientific models, historical knowledge, writing, disciplined attention, physical competence, collaboration, self-regulation and occupational skill. This includes both knowledge that changes what a person can perceive and practice that changes what a person can do. A learner cannot evaluate an AI-generated historical claim without historical knowledge, estimate a numerical output without number sense, or design a sound experiment without causal reasoning.

2.2 Common membership and civic formation

Schools create shared reference points, relationships across families and practice living under common rules. They transmit language, history, norms, civic procedures and sometimes contested national narratives. This role can support democratic participation and social trust; it can also become coercive assimilation or ideological control. The correct response is pluralistic civic education and transparent curriculum governance, not pretending education is culturally neutral. 19 20 72

2.3 Care infrastructure

For many children, school is also supervision, food, routine, disability support, counselling, health screening, protection from abuse and access to trusted adults. In low-resource settings, water, sanitation, school meals and safe transport can be preconditions for attendance. Health-promoting-school frameworks therefore treat nutrition, safety, physical activity and mental health as part of learning infrastructure rather than optional extras. 77 78

2.4 Coordination, selection and signalling

Education coordinates transitions into higher study, regulated professions and labour markets. Grades and credentials reduce information costs for employers and admissions systems. They can certify real competence, signal traits such as persistence, or ration scarce opportunities. Spence’s signalling model explains why credentials may retain market value even when some course content is not used directly. The public challenge is to make signals more truthful and less costly—not to assume that every signal is empty. 24 13

The core policy mistake is to declare one function—test performance, employability, happiness or self-expression—the sole purpose of education. A legitimate system must protect a common capability floor, support individual development above that floor, maintain care and civic infrastructure, and create fair, transparent pathways into adulthood.

Which problems are educational—and which are not

Education can mitigate but cannot independently solve poverty, housing instability, family violence, public-health shortages, discrimination or weak labour demand. When food insecurity, chronic illness or unsafe transport causes absence, a new curriculum is not the first remedy. Conversely, systems sometimes attribute instructional failure to poverty when better sequencing, materials and tutoring can substantially improve learning among disadvantaged students. The practical rule is to identify the nearest modifiable cause: instruction when the learner has not been taught effectively; social support when the learner cannot access instruction; labour-market reform when qualifications do not lead to opportunity.

3. Historical Diagnosis

The “factory model” thesis is a useful metaphor and a poor origin story

The popular claim that modern schools were deliberately designed to create factory workers compresses two centuries of diverse institutional history into one motive. Nineteenth-century European states expanded schooling as part of nation-building; compulsory schooling in the United States also helped assimilate mass immigration; religious bodies had long organized instruction; reformers sought literacy, social order, civic participation and child welfare. Industrialization strengthened demand for literacy and administrative uniformity, but the historical record does not support one coordinated design to train children for assembly lines. 19 20 21

What the metaphor gets right

  • Batching and throughput. Age-graded classrooms, fixed schedules and standardized curricula made large systems governable.
  • Division of labour. Teachers, subjects, periods, inspectors and administrators created specialized roles.
  • Standardized inputs and outputs. Textbooks, examinations and credentials enabled mobility and state oversight.
  • Behavioural discipline. Punctuality, attendance, quiet work and compliance were compatible with bureaucratic and industrial organizations.
  • Efficiency logic. Reform debates increasingly borrowed production metaphors and sought scale, comparability and cost control.

What it gets wrong

  • Age grading was not only industrial. Historical research shows both bureaucratic and pedagogical reasons for grouping children by age, and adoption was uneven across Western Europe. 22
  • Mass schooling preceded or differed from factory employment in many places. Religious, civic and colonial systems had distinct purposes.
  • Schools often promoted mobility away from manual labour. Academic credentials became routes into administration and professions.
  • Progressive, vocational, classical and child-centred traditions coexisted with bureaucratic schooling. The history is a contested ecology, not a single machine.

Colonial and examination inheritances

Colonial systems often used schooling to create administrative intermediaries, spread imperial languages and establish hierarchies between academic and local knowledge. Macaulay’s 1835 Minute is a primary example of elite English-language educational policy in British India, but it should not be treated as a complete account of Indian education before or after colonial rule. Independent states later adapted inherited examinations and civil-service structures for nation-building and mass access. 23

Three structures that have outlived their original constraints

  1. Time-based progression. Students receive similar instructional time but leave with very different mastery. Time should remain scheduled; advancement within domains should respond more to demonstrated learning.
  2. The one-shot assessment economy. Terminal exams reward performance on a narrow day and encourage expensive coaching. More systems should combine secure exams with cumulative demonstrations, reassessment and moderated portfolios.
  3. The degree as a monolithic package. Degrees remain valuable for broad formation and regulated professions, but many learners need stackable, transferable modules linked to supervised work and independent competence testing.

The historical lesson is conservative and reformist at once: institutions persist because they solve coordination problems, but mechanisms can become detached from purposes. Reform should preserve universal access, common standards and public accountability while updating how time, evidence and pathways are organized.

Bottom line: the most transferable system features are not brand names. They are coherent curriculum, teachers with usable materials and time, early identification of gaps, adequate student support, credible standards, respected plural pathways and institutions capable of implementing the same policy for more than one political cycle.

5. Why People Opt Out

Opting out is usually a search for fit, control or lower risk—not proof that one alternative is superior

“Opting out” covers very different decisions: a family removing a bullied child from school; a gifted learner seeking faster pacing; a religious family choosing home education; a teenager entering an apprenticeship; an adult choosing a short credential; or a student declining an expensive degree with uncertain completion. These choices share dissatisfaction with the default, but they do not share one cause, one social class or one outcome.

The strongest motives, separated from the narratives built around them

Safety, bullying and psychological fit

For some families, home education or a small alternative setting is a rational response to bullying, school refusal, sensory overload, chronic anxiety, discrimination or an environment that repeatedly fails to accommodate disability. These are not trivial lifestyle preferences. However, removing a child can relieve immediate distress without treating anxiety, restoring peer confidence or ensuring specialist support. The best decision process includes clinical or educational assessment, a reintegration option, the child’s own voice and an explicit social-development plan. 11 12 16

Academic pace and personalization

Families often want slower pacing for gaps, faster pacing for advanced learners, more time on interests or fewer transitions. Home and microschool environments can make flexible pacing easier. Their advantage is organizational: fewer students, fewer timetable constraints and closer adult observation. Their risk is curricular blind spots and uneven expertise. Personalization should therefore mean variable time and support against a clear entitlement, not an entirely private curriculum with no external benchmark.

Values, religion and institutional trust

Some families seek religious formation, ideological consistency or protection from curricula they see as hostile. Liberal systems should allow pluralism, but children also have interests in broad knowledge, contact with differing viewpoints, protection from abuse and the future ability to choose their own commitments. UNESCO’s rights-based review argues for proportionate registration, minimum curricular expectations, child welfare oversight and access to assessment rather than either prohibition or total deregulation. 18

Flexibility, family life and place

Travel, elite sport, disability, remote residence, family work and irregular schedules can make conventional attendance impractical. Hybrid and online models can widen access. Yet flexibility transfers planning, motivation, equipment and supervision costs to the household. The model works best when families can combine asynchronous work with reliable adult instruction, peer contact and external feedback.

Cost and uncertain value in higher education

Young adults may choose direct employment, a gap year, an apprenticeship or a shorter credential because tuition, living costs and foregone earnings are substantial. This is not evidence that degrees have ceased to pay. OECD data still show average employment and earnings advantages, but the average hides non-completion, field differences, local labour demand and institutional quality. The rational reform is transparent programme-level data and plural pathways—not marketing every learner into college or telling every learner that college is a scam. 13 68 70

A rights-and-quality test for any opt-out pathway

An alternative should be judged by five questions rather than by its label:

  1. Capability: Is the learner acquiring a broad foundation and genuine domain competence?
  2. Autonomy: Does the learner—not only the parent or founder—gain increasing voice and future options?
  3. Relationships: Are there reliable peers, mentors, trusted adults and opportunities to cooperate across difference?
  4. Protection: Are safeguarding, disability access, health and complaints procedures credible?
  5. Portability: Can learning be independently verified and carried into further study, employment or licensure?

A conventional school can fail all five tests; a home or microschool can pass them. The reverse is also true.

6. Science of Learning

The durable-learning sequence

The science of learning does not prescribe one branded model. It supports a sequence that is broadly robust:

Activate prior knowledge → explain and model → guide practice → retrieve and space → give actionable feedback → vary contexts → require explanation and application → increase independence → revisit after delay.

This sequence reflects three distinctions. First, performance during practice is not the same as later retention: hints, examples and AI can make today’s work fluent while leaving little independent capability. Second, novices and experts need different support: an open problem that stimulates an expert can overload a beginner. Third, near transfer is easier than far transfer: critical thinking usually depends on substantial knowledge of the domain being judged.

Popular Learning Advice That Is Weak, Misleading, or Overstated

  1. “Teach each student in their visual, auditory or kinaesthetic learning style.” People have preferences, and material should be represented in ways appropriate to the content. But controlled evidence does not support matching instruction to a diagnosed style to improve learning. A map is useful for geography because geography is spatial, not because a learner was labelled visual. 52

  2. “The left brain is logical and the right brain is creative.” Brain functions are distributed and coordinated; educational applications of this dichotomy are neuromyths. The same is true of claims that learners use only 10% of the brain or that a branded exercise “integrates” hemispheres. 53

  3. “Discovery always beats direct teaching.” Minimal guidance often overloads novices. Inquiry becomes effective when prior knowledge, prompts, examples and feedback make the search productive. 32 34

  4. “Memorization is the opposite of understanding.” Rote repetition without meaning is weak, but fluent recall frees working memory and supplies the material for reasoning. Understanding determines what should be remembered; memory makes understanding usable later.

  5. “Growth mindset interventions transform achievement.” Beliefs about malleability can matter, and well-targeted interventions sometimes help lower-achieving students in supportive contexts. Meta-analytic average achievement effects are small and sensitive to study quality. Mindset cannot compensate for unclear teaching, unsafe classrooms or missing prerequisites. 51

  6. “A student who enjoyed the lesson learned more.” Enjoyment, belonging and curiosity support persistence, but immediate fluency and positive feeling can coexist with weak retention. Measure later recall, transfer and independent performance.

  7. “Struggle is always desirable.” Productive difficulty strengthens learning only when the learner has a plausible path, feedback and enough prior knowledge. Confusion without resolution teaches avoidance or misconceptions.

  8. “Digital natives naturally know how to learn with technology.” Familiarity with apps is not information literacy, source evaluation, programming, privacy judgment or self-regulation. These require explicit teaching.

  9. “Personalization software automatically individualizes learning.” Systems can adjust item difficulty, but they often personalize practice rather than goals, explanations, relationships or meaning. Black-box adaptation may also narrow the curriculum and reduce teacher visibility.

  10. “Neuroscience has proved the new pedagogy.” Neural findings can explain mechanisms and constraints, but most classroom decisions still depend more directly on behavioural evidence. Brain images do not upgrade a weak educational study into strong causal evidence.

What the science does—and does not—settle

The evidence strongly supports cumulative knowledge, active retrieval, spacing, guided practice, feedback and adaptation to current understanding. It does not identify one ideal timetable, curriculum canon, grading system or institutional form. Motivation, culture and relationships matter, but they interact with instruction rather than replacing it. The practical science is therefore less glamorous than most reform brands: design tasks that make learners think about the right content, return to it after delay, see errors, correct them, and use the knowledge in increasingly independent situations.

7. Lessons From Exceptional Learners

Exceptional lives reveal design features, not recipes

Biographies are useful for discovering mechanisms—mentorship, notebooks, real work, access to tools—but they are poor evidence for average treatment effects. Accomplished people are selected after success; failures with similar habits disappear from the record. Their accounts may simplify childhood, and unusual ability, family support, wealth, discrimination, historical opportunity or sheer luck can dominate the result. The recurring patterns

Across very different lives, eight patterns recur often enough to inform institutional design:

  1. Large stores of knowledge. Exceptional work rarely emerges from generic creativity detached from a field.
  2. Sustained attention to self-chosen questions. The key is not unlimited choice, but deep ownership after sufficient exposure.
  3. Notebooks, sketches, prototypes and external memory. Ideas become inspectable and revisable.
  4. Mentors and intellectual peers. Independence usually grows inside a community of standards.
  5. Access to tools, texts, workshops, laboratories or real data. Curiosity becomes capability when material access exists.
  6. Consequential projects. Publishing, building, calculating a flight path or solving a public problem raises standards beyond teacher approval.
  7. Cross-domain contact. New combinations matter, but they are built from actual domains rather than vague interdisciplinarity.
  8. Repeated failure with feedback. Productive failure occurred within long practice and support, not as abandonment.

What biographies do not justify

They do not justify designing schools only for unusually motivated students, abolishing explicit instruction, expecting parents to recreate elite intellectual homes, or treating adversity as character-building. Many exceptional learners benefited profoundly from formal schools, universities, apprenticeships and public institutions. The transferable lesson is narrower: give more ordinary learners some of the conditions historically reserved for the fortunate—expert mentorship, tools, sustained projects, intellectual peers, feedback and real responsibility—while preserving systematic foundations.

8. Education for the AI Age

AI changes the economics of tasks before it settles the future of occupations

The most defensible near-term claim is not that “AI will take all jobs,” nor that nothing fundamental changes. The ILO’s 2025 global exposure index estimates that roughly one in four workers is in an occupation with some generative-AI exposure, while about 3.3% of global employment is in the highest-exposure category. Exposure is higher in high-income countries and clerical occupations; task transformation is more likely than complete occupational replacement. Employer surveys anticipate rapid skill change, but such forecasts reflect expectations rather than settled outcomes. 57 80

Experiments already show a “jagged frontier.” Generative AI improved speed and quality in professional writing tasks and helped less-experienced customer-support workers disproportionately. Yet experienced open-source developers in one 2025 randomized study were slower using early-2025 tools on familiar repositories, despite believing they were faster. Effects depend on task, model, user skill, context and the cost of checking. 61 62 63

Capabilities becoming less scarce

  • Routine information retrieval and first-pass summarization
  • Formulaic emails, reports and standard presentation copy
  • Boilerplate or familiar code generation
  • Translation and low-stakes content adaptation
  • Basic image, audio and design production
  • Standard procedural guidance and office administration
  • Patterned homework that can be completed from the prompt alone

What everyone should know by approximately 2035

A broad foundation remains more—not less—important because AI increases the volume of claims a person must judge. Every learner should leave secondary education with:

  • Language and literature: fluent reading, clear writing, oral communication, interpretation and exposure to significant texts.
  • Mathematics and statistics: number sense, algebraic and geometric reasoning, probability, data interpretation, estimation and model limitations.
  • Science: core explanatory models in life, physical and earth sciences; experimental design; measurement; causal inference and uncertainty.
  • History and civics: chronology, institutions, comparative political systems, rights, law, media, colonialism, economic history and reasoned disagreement.
  • Economics, finance and legal basics: compound interest, taxes, insurance, contracts, consumer rights, labour relations, public budgets and risk.
  • Computing and AI: algorithms, data, programming fundamentals, networks, cybersecurity, model training and inference, bias, privacy and evaluation.
  • Health and human development: nutrition, sleep, physical activity, sexual health, mental health, first aid, relationships and help-seeking.
  • Arts, design and craft: making, aesthetic judgment, visual and musical literacy, iterative design and appreciation of cultural traditions.

This is not a call for encyclopedic coverage. It is a common conceptual map deep enough to support later specialization and to prevent AI-generated fragments from becoming the learner’s only worldview.

What everyone should be able to do

By the end of secondary education, a learner should be able to:

  1. Read a demanding text, summarize its argument and identify what would change their mind.
  2. Write and speak clearly for a real audience without AI, then improve the work with tools while preserving authorship and accountability.
  3. Estimate, calculate and interpret quantities; detect orders-of-magnitude errors and misleading graphs.
  4. Formulate a testable question, collect or locate evidence, distinguish correlation from causation and state uncertainty.
  5. Trace a consequential claim to a primary or authoritative source and explain why that source deserves trust.
  6. Use an AI system to generate, analyze or automate work; document the process; test the output; and know when not to use it.
  7. Plan and complete a multi-week project with milestones, collaborators, revision and a public product.
  8. Perform at least one substantial physical, technical, artistic, caring or craft activity to an externally judged standard.
  9. Participate in disagreement, negotiate roles, give feedback and repair a conflict.
  10. Diagnose a personal learning gap and build a credible plan to close it.

The developmental principle is simple: the younger and less knowledgeable the learner, the more AI should be delayed, bounded and mediated. The goal is not fear; it is to prevent a tool that predicts language from becoming a substitute attachment figure, answer machine or unearned cognitive prosthesis.

What should remain unaided human practice

Schools should protect recurring “closed-tool” practice in:

  • sustained reading of complete texts;
  • foundational handwriting, spelling and composition;
  • mental arithmetic, estimation and algebraic manipulation;
  • explanation from memory and oral reasoning;
  • drawing observations, laboratory measurement and physical making;
  • basic coding, debugging and algorithm tracing;
  • live interpersonal communication, debate, performance and care;
  • secure cumulative assessments of essential knowledge.

The purpose is not to simulate a world without tools. Pilots still train unaided procedures even though aircraft have automation because independent competence is required for understanding, monitoring and failure. Students need the same cognitive fallback.

What should be done with AI

AI is most educationally useful when it increases feedback, variation, access or production without removing the central thought:

  • generate additional practice after the teacher defines the target;
  • give hints through a graduated ladder rather than full answers;
  • simulate clients, historical choices, patients, systems or languages;
  • offer translation and accessibility support;
  • compare multiple explanations and ask learners to critique them;
  • provide first-pass feedback that must be applied and defended;
  • help analyze datasets, prototype designs and test code after fundamentals;
  • support teachers with planning, differentiation and administrative drafting under privacy rules.

Assessment must change before AI policy can work

A credible AI-era assessment system should combine five forms of evidence:

  1. Secure unaided core checks. Short, cumulative tests, oral questions and practical demonstrations establish what the learner can do without assistance.
  2. AI-permitted authentic work. Students use contemporary tools on complex tasks where tool choice, verification and outcome quality matter.
  3. Process evidence. Notes, drafts, version history, experiment logs, code commits, source trails and decision records show how work developed.
  4. Oral defence and transfer. A learner explains choices, responds to counterexamples and solves a related problem live.
  5. Longitudinal portfolio with moderation. Selected work demonstrates growth, but external rubrics and sampling protect reliability.

“No AI” and “AI allowed” are both too crude. Every assignment should declare one of four modes: closed, hints only, collaborative AI with disclosure, or AI-required professional workflow.

How to prevent AI from weakening foundational cognition

The strongest practical safeguards are task designs rather than surveillance:

  • Attempt before assistance: require a plan, first solution or prediction before opening the tool.
  • Hint ladder: AI asks questions or exposes one step at a time.
  • Retrieval after assistance: close the tool and reconstruct the solution.
  • Comparison: evaluate at least two outputs or one output against an authoritative source.
  • Error hunt: include intentionally flawed AI work and reward detection.
  • Transfer test: assess a new but structurally related problem without AI.
  • Process disclosure: show prompts, edits, rejected outputs and verification.
  • Human stakes: require live presentation, peer responsibility, physical production or client feedback.
  • Tool-free intervals: preserve days, lessons and examination components in which students practise independent cognition.

The controlled mathematics evidence illustrates why. Unrestricted AI raised immediate exercise performance while reducing later unaided performance; a guardrailed tutor avoided the harm. Separately, Tutor CoPilot improved student mastery when it helped human tutors respond more effectively, with the largest gains among lower-rated tutors. The better architecture is often AI behind or beside a responsible adult, not AI as the adult. 59 60

Governance: what an institution must decide before procurement

Every school, university or ministry should require:

  • a documented instructional purpose and comparison with a non-AI option;
  • age-appropriate data minimization, retention and deletion rules;
  • prohibition on training vendor models with student work without explicit authority;
  • accessibility and bias testing;
  • teacher and student ability to contest automated recommendations;
  • transparency about model version, material changes and outages;
  • exportable records and avoidance of vendor lock-in;
  • independent evaluation of learning, workload, equity and unintended effects;
  • a sunset clause if evidence does not justify renewal.

AI should be treated as fallible infrastructure inside education, not as the purpose of education.

Celebrated failures and “bad buys” matter as much as success stories

Three recurring failure patterns cut across models:

  1. Technology without an instructional model. Devices and platforms do not improve learning merely by being present. The Global Education Evidence Advisory Panel identifies hardware-only programmes as poor investments relative to structured pedagogy, targeted instruction and other supported interventions. 55
  2. Training without operating conditions. Teaching at the Right Level failed in early government scale-ups when teachers received training but no protected period, monitoring or practical support. When those conditions changed, implementation and learning improved. 54
  3. Autonomy without standards or support. Self-paced, project-based, online and competency programmes can work for self-regulated learners, but weak scaffolding often widens gaps because advantaged students possess more background knowledge, quiet space, adult help and planning skill.

The cross-model synthesis

The strongest alternatives converge on a common design:

  • a coherent knowledge and skill progression;
  • substantial learner choice inside clear boundaries;
  • long periods for focused work;
  • frequent expert feedback;
  • authentic products, workplaces or audiences;
  • flexible time and support where mastery differs;
  • strong peer and mentor relationships;
  • independent evidence that learning occurred;
  • public safeguards and portable pathways.

The model label is secondary. An “innovative” school without these conditions can be worse than a conventional school; a conventional school that adds them can become genuinely transformative.

11. Minimum Viable Reform Package

The smallest coherent package that should move outcomes

A minimum viable reform package is not the cheapest list of activities. It is the smallest set in which each element solves a failure created by another: diagnostics identify gaps; curriculum and teaching address them; tutoring adds time; assessment checks retention and transfer; teacher collaboration maintains quality; authentic work develops application; and student support keeps learners present.

11.1 Conventional public school

Core package

  1. Define a limited set of essential knowledge and skills for each term.
  2. Run a short baseline diagnostic and six-week mastery/reassessment cycle.
  3. Use daily retrieval, weekly cumulative review and worked-example fading.
  4. Provide three weekly tutoring sessions for the lowest-performing 15–20% in literacy or mathematics, aligned to class content.
  5. Protect one 60–90-minute weekly application block using science, humanities, arts, craft or community problems.
  6. Implement the four-mode AI assessment protocol and at least one secure unaided component per major unit.
  7. Give every student a named adviser; contact families quickly after emerging absence.
  8. Replace one recurring administrative meeting with subject/grade collaboration and coaching.

Measures after one year: delayed retention, common assessment growth, gap closure, tutoring dosage, attendance, student belonging, teacher workload and project quality. Stop or redesign any component that adds more than two hours weekly teacher burden without evidence of student benefit.

11.2 Private or independent school

Use the public-school package, then exploit greater timetable flexibility rather than merely adding enrichment:

  • create mixed-age mastery workshops for one block daily while preserving age-based advisory and social groups;
  • contract a diverse external mentor network and guarantee every student a substantive community, research or enterprise project;
  • publish externally moderated learning and wellbeing outcomes, not only university placement;
  • cap reliance on homework and parental production by providing tools and project time in school;
  • use tuition assistance and transport to prevent innovative programmes from becoming social enclaves.

The main risk is innovation theatre: attractive studios, technology and projects that do not produce stronger knowledge or more equitable access.

11.3 Resource-constrained school

Prioritize high-return basics and avoid hardware-first reform:

  1. Use simple paper or oral diagnostics for reading and arithmetic.
  2. Protect a daily 45–60-minute Teaching-at-the-Right-Level block with flexible, temporary groups.
  3. Provide clear teacher guides, locally understandable examples and low-cost manipulatives.
  4. Use pair reading, peer tutoring and choral/all-student response to increase practice.
  5. Track attendance and visit/contact families after repeated absence; connect to meals, health or transport support where possible.
  6. Preserve daily play and movement and one weekly local problem/project using community knowledge and available materials.
  7. Use shared basic phones or offline resources only when they solve a defined access problem; do not make AI a prerequisite.
  8. Hold monthly teacher practice meetings focused on one routine and student work.

Measures: percentage of learners moving at least one reading/arithmetic level, attendance, instructional time, teacher use of materials, cost per learner and subgroup participation. 54 55 77

11.4 University programme

  1. Publish programme-level completion, time, total cost, work placement and employment/earnings data with appropriate caveats.
  2. Identify the knowledge and performances graduates must demonstrate; map every course to them.
  3. Use secure cumulative concept checks plus AI-permitted authentic projects with process logs and oral defence.
  4. Replace some large lecture time with problem sessions, tutorials and peer instruction.
  5. Require a supervised work, research, clinical, community or entrepreneurial capstone.
  6. Create stackable exit awards and guaranteed credit transfer so partial completion retains value.
  7. Teach professional AI use, data privacy, verification and accountability inside the discipline—not as a generic one-hour module.
  8. Review courses with high failure or low value using student work, not only satisfaction surveys.

Measures: programme completion, concept retention, transfer task performance, placement quality, debt/cost, graduate outcomes, employer/supervisor evaluations and equity gaps.

11.5 Homeschool or microschool environment

  1. Establish a broad curriculum entitlement and yearly external reading, writing and mathematics benchmark.
  2. Separate the parent/founder’s preferences from the learner’s developing voice through regular child-led reviews and an external adviser.
  3. Schedule stable peer groups for collaboration, sport, arts, debate and contact across difference.
  4. Use qualified tutors or community specialists for subjects beyond adult expertise.
  5. Require a portfolio plus externally moderated samples and oral explanation.
  6. Document safeguarding, complaints, emergency, disability and online-privacy procedures.
  7. Limit AI to supervised, declared modes; protect daily independent reading, writing, mathematics and physical making.
  8. Maintain a transition plan into examinations, further education, apprenticeship or school re-entry.

Measures: external growth, curriculum breadth, learner wellbeing and voice, peer/contact hours, adult workload, safeguarding incidents and successful transitions.

14. Red-Team Analysis

The strongest defence of conventional education

Standardization exists because arbitrary discretion is worse

Common curricula, age cohorts, school calendars and examinations coordinate millions of people. External tests can reveal that a district is failing disadvantaged children and can protect students from locally inflated grades or patronage. A system based entirely on portfolios, recommendations and interviews may favour families with social capital, coaching and attractive extracurricular narratives.

Revision to the agenda: preserve a common entitlement and secure external sampling. Add authentic evidence; do not replace comparability with impressionistic judgment.

Teacher-led instruction remains efficient and humane

A knowledgeable teacher can explain a concept to 25 learners, notice confusion, establish norms and create a shared intellectual experience. One-to-one personalization is expensive, and individualized screens can isolate students. Direct instruction has strong evidence; novices often do not know what they need to discover. 33 34

Revision: the reform model uses concise teacher explanation, worked examples and guided practice as the default introduction to new material. Autonomy and projects increase with knowledge.

Content knowledge is the infrastructure of reasoning

Generic “critical thinking,” creativity and media literacy do not float above subject matter. A student cannot verify a medical claim, interpret a historical analogy or debug generated code without domain knowledge. Search and AI reduce retrieval cost but increase the importance of knowing what questions to ask and what answers are plausible.

Revision: maintain a rich common curriculum and regular unaided retrieval. Project quality never substitutes for individual knowledge.

Schools cannot be built only around exceptional or self-directed learners

Many students arrive tired, anxious, unmotivated or without adult support. Some need structure before they can exercise agency. A system that assumes every learner can design a curriculum, manage a project and solicit mentors will reward those who already possess the habits it claims to teach.

Revision: use bounded choice, explicit routines, adviser support and gradual release. All open-ended tasks include milestones, models and individual accountability.

Reform repeatedly disappoints because implementation is the intervention

A promising pilot often relies on charismatic founders, volunteer effort, smaller groups or intensive coaching. Scale dilutes training, changes the student population and collides with schedules, contracts and accountability. Teaching at the Right Level and teacher-coaching evidence both show weaker effects when support is reduced. 41 54

Revision: every reform includes workload, cost, fidelity, stop rules and an evaluation metric. National scale follows independent replication, not launch publicity.

AI-centred education could worsen both cognition and inequality

Affluent learners may receive human mentoring plus AI, while disadvantaged learners receive AI instead of teachers. Vendors may extract sensitive data, personalized systems may fragment common knowledge, and answer engines can remove the practice that creates independent skill. Early evidence already demonstrates that assistance can improve current work while harming later unaided performance. 57 58 59

Revision: AI is not a substitute staffing model. Protect human contact, common curriculum, closed-tool practice, privacy, auditability and equal access; measure learning after tool removal.

Alternatives may not scale and may weaken shared society

Homeschooling and microschools can fit individual families, but mass adoption could increase segregation by class, religion or ideology and weaken access to specialists, arts, sports and child protection. Employer-led pathways can sort disadvantaged students into narrower futures.

Revision: parallel alternatives must meet public standards for capability, learner voice, safeguarding, non-discrimination, external assessment and pathway portability.

More purposes can mean less focus

Adding financial literacy, AI ethics, wellbeing, entrepreneurship, coding, climate, media literacy and project work can overload the timetable and teachers. A reform agenda can become the very curriculum bloat it criticizes.

Revision: integrate applications into strong disciplines, identify a small set of common outcomes, stop low-value tasks and require a time budget for every addition.

What survives the red team

The red-team case strengthens rather than defeats a balanced reform model:

  • common knowledge and variable time/support;
  • teacher authority and increasing student agency;
  • secure assessment and authentic performance;
  • shared schools and regulated plural pathways;
  • AI-supported work and unaided cognitive practice;
  • national or provincial standards and local adaptation;
  • disciplined implementation and controlled experimentation.

The false binary is the main enemy. Durable reform combines institutions’ coordination strengths with a more truthful model of how people learn and grow.

What We Should Stop Doing

  1. Stop moving students through essential sequences when diagnostics show missing prerequisites without providing additional time and instruction.
  2. Stop using one exam, one grade average, one portfolio or one algorithm as a complete measure of a person.
  3. Stop assigning large volumes of predictable homework that AI can complete and that teachers cannot meaningfully review.
  4. Stop introducing novices to complex domains through minimally guided discovery and calling confusion “agency.”
  5. Stop treating memorization and understanding as enemies; stop assessing recall without explanation and application.
  6. Stop buying devices, platforms or AI licences without a defined instructional mechanism, baseline, privacy review, evaluation and sunset clause.
  7. Stop one-off professional-development days disconnected from curriculum, observation, rehearsal and feedback.
  8. Stop treating vocational education as a lower-status destination for students judged “not academic.”
  9. Stop requiring degrees for jobs when validated skills, supervised experience or licensure can establish competence more directly.
  10. Stop grading presentation polish, compliance and group products as though they prove individual understanding.
  11. Stop teaching learning styles, left/right-brain claims and other neuromyths as scientific fact.
  12. Stop adding priorities to the school day without removing lower-value work and calculating teacher time.
  13. Stop using punitive attendance responses before investigating health, bullying, disability, transport and family barriers.
  14. Stop framing AI policy as a binary ban or permission; specify the cognitive work the student must still own.
  15. Stop publishing institution-level success claims without denominator, selection, non-completion, subgroup and comparison data.

What We Must Preserve

  1. A common entitlement to powerful knowledge, literacy and numeracy.
  2. Qualified teachers who explain, model, question, care and exercise judgment.
  3. Shared institutions where young people encounter peers beyond the family and learn to live with difference.
  4. Routines, deadlines, sustained effort and the discipline of doing difficult work.
  5. External standards and secure assessment that make inequity visible and credentials portable.
  6. Quiet, extended reading, writing, calculation and reflection without tool mediation.
  7. Arts, music, physical education, laboratories, craft, play and contact with nature.
  8. School meals, counselling, disability support, safeguarding and trusted adults.
  9. Deep disciplinary expertise and professional standards—not only generic “skills.”
  10. Equitable public funding and the principle that family income should not determine educational possibility.
  11. The broad intellectual purposes of universities: inquiry, scholarship, civic debate and formation beyond immediate job tasks.
  12. A shared civic and historical conversation that permits disagreement rather than ideological isolation.

What We Need to Experiment With

These ideas are strategically promising but insufficiently proven for universal adoption:

  • guardrailed AI tutors that require attempts, hints, explanations and transfer tests;
  • mixed-age mastery blocks combined with stable age-based communities;
  • public microschool networks with common curriculum, safeguarding and external assessment;
  • independently validated, stackable credentials accepted by multiple employers and universities;
  • portfolio-plus-viva systems that are reliable, affordable and resistant to AI or parent substitution;
  • later secondary-school schedules integrated with transport, family and extracurricular constraints;
  • community mentor guilds and maker laboratories available to all schools;
  • lifelong-learning accounts with paid study time and strict provider-quality rules;
  • adaptive pacing systems that preserve learner privacy and teacher visibility;
  • admissions and hiring trials using secure work samples rather than default degree filters.

Every experiment should use a comparison, a predeclared outcome, subgroup analysis, workload and cost data, ethical monitoring and a stopping rule.

An Education System Worth Building

An education system for the AI age should operate by ten institutional principles:

  1. Common entitlement, variable support. Every learner receives a broad foundation; time, explanation and practice vary until essential mastery is achieved.
  2. Knowledge before and alongside independence. Teachers make the structure of a field visible, then release responsibility through increasingly open problems.
  3. Memory, understanding and application are one cycle. Learners retrieve, explain, use, receive feedback and revisit after delay.
  4. Human relationships are core infrastructure. Technology may expand feedback and access; it does not replace teachers, peers, mentors, care or accountable adults.
  5. AI augments work but never owns responsibility. Students disclose use, verify outputs and demonstrate what they can do when the tool is removed.
  6. Assessment samples different kinds of capability. Secure knowledge, authentic performance, process, collaboration and oral defence each reveal something different.
  7. Every learner does consequential work. Projects, placements, laboratories, performances, service and enterprise connect knowledge to standards outside school.
  8. Pathways are plural and permeable. Academic, vocational, artistic, technical and professional routes share a common foundation and allow later movement.
  9. Care and equity are design constraints. Food, safety, disability, mental health, transport, devices and adult time determine whether a pedagogy is reachable.
  10. Reform is a disciplined learning process. Pilot, compare, measure retention and transfer, publish failures, reduce workload, scale only what survives real institutions.

The goal is not a school that maximizes student choice, test scores, employability or AI use in isolation. It is a public architecture that produces people who know enough to judge, can do enough to contribute, care enough to act responsibly, and can continue learning without surrendering their agency to an institution or a machine.

Appendix A. Research Limits, Open Questions and Verification

Research limitations

  1. The newest comparative achievement data lag current classrooms. PISA 2022 is the latest complete PISA outcomes cycle available at this report’s 19 July 2026 cut-off; PISA 2025 initial results are scheduled for 8 September 2026. National assessments and administrative data partially update the picture but are not directly comparable across countries. 1 90
  2. International assessments measure selected competencies, not the full purpose of education. PISA and national tests are valuable for literacy, numeracy and applied knowledge; they do not directly measure moral character, creativity across domains, civic conduct, practical craft or lifelong flourishing.
  3. PISA and most international data are observational. Country characteristics correlate with outcomes but do not prove that importing a policy would cause the same result elsewhere. Culture, migration, selection, tutoring, demographics and implementation interact.
  4. India’s ASER sample is rural. It is exceptionally useful for basic reading and arithmetic trends, but it should not be treated as a direct estimate for urban or private-school populations. 5
  5. Meta-analyses combine unlike interventions. Average effect sizes can hide subject, age, duration, outcome, publication-bias and implementation differences. They guide priorities; they do not guarantee a local effect.
  6. Many learning studies use short outcomes. Long-term retention, far transfer, employment, wellbeing and civic effects are measured less often than immediate tests.
  7. Homeschooling, microschool and alternative-school comparisons have severe selection problems. Registration definitions vary, and families differ systematically. This report therefore avoids claiming average causal superiority. 17 18 85
  8. AI evidence is young and perishable. Results depend on model version, task, interface, guardrails and user skill. A 2025 study can become technologically dated before its institutional lesson—such as the performance/learning distinction—loses relevance. The OECD’s 2026 synthesis is useful, but long-run developmental evidence remains unavailable. 59 63 91
  9. Labour forecasts are scenarios, not destiny. Exposure measures describe task overlap, while employer surveys describe expectations. Policy, adoption cost, liability, demand and new tasks determine actual employment. 57 80
  10. Exceptional-learner cases are deliberately non-representative. They identify potentially transferable conditions but cannot estimate effects for ordinary students.
  11. Cost and feasibility ratings are relative. A “low-cost” reform in a wealthy system may be unaffordable in a school lacking toilets, books or reliable staff. Local unit costs, language and institutional capacity must be modelled before implementation.
  12. This report is broad rather than jurisdictionally legal. Home-education, privacy, disability, labour, credential and safeguarding rules require country- and province/state-specific legal review.

Questions that could materially change the agenda

  • Do PISA 2025 results show continued lower-tail decline, recovery or a new pattern after 2022?
  • Which guardrail designs improve unaided learning across age groups and subjects, not only task completion?
  • Can AI-supported tutoring preserve human contact and produce cost-effective gains at public-system scale?
  • Which portfolio and oral-assessment designs achieve acceptable reliability without unsustainable teacher workload?
  • How can flexible mastery pacing operate without creating permanent low-status tracks?
  • Which microcredentials predict work performance and remain portable across employers and universities?
  • What funding and supervision models produce high-quality apprenticeships during economic downturns?
  • Which phone and attention policies reduce total harmful use rather than moving it outside school?
  • What combinations of curriculum, tutoring, attendance support and health services most improve outcomes for disadvantaged students?
  • How do reforms affect learners with disabilities, neurodivergent students, multilingual learners and those already performing far above grade level?

Final verification pass

The final pass applied four checks.

Original-source tracing. Headline statistics were traced to original OECD, government, multilateral or study reports: PISA, ASER, NAEP, England’s Department for Education, NCES, Statistics Canada, WHO, ILO and primary/meta-analytic papers. Secondary reporting was not used for headline prevalence where an original source was available.

Causal-language audit. Randomized and quasi-experimental evidence is described as effects; cross-sectional or administrative associations are described as associations or patterns. Country comparisons, biographies, phone-policy research and most homeschooling outcomes are not presented as causal.

Duplication and denominator audit. Pandemic and pre-pandemic homeschooling estimates are not combined into one trend; PISA points are not translated into arbitrary “years of learning”; effect sizes from different designs are not directly ranked; rural ASER findings are labelled rural.

Recommendation-strength audit. High-confidence recommendations rely on converging evidence—foundational mastery, coherent teaching, retrieval/spacing, tutoring, formative assessment and teacher coaching. Lower-confidence proposals—microschools, AI cooperatives, flexible age progression and alternative credentials—are placed in pilots with stop rules rather than presented as settled policy.

  23. Bastani et al., “Generative AI without guardrails can harm learning” (2025). Direct experimental evidence for the report’s central distinction between assisted performance and later unaided learning. Source
  24. Wang et al., Tutor CoPilot (2024 preprint). A preregistered live tutoring trial showing that AI can improve learning when it supports a human tutor’s pedagogy rather than giving students answers. Source
  25. OECD, Digital Education Outlook 2026. The most current authoritative synthesis at the research cut-off; emphasizes that generative AI can support learning only under clear teaching principles and warns against “false mastery.” Source

  27. Campbell Collaboration Montessori systematic review (2023). Finds positive average effects while documenting heterogeneity and fidelity concerns; supports taking the operating features seriously without treating a label as causal proof. Source
  28. Goodyear et al., SMART Schools phone-policy study (2025). Shows that restrictive school policies reduced in-school use but were not associated with better overall wellbeing or total use, arguing for attention rules embedded in a broader family/platform strategy. Source
