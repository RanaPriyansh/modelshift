Decision in one sentence

The best thing to build is Forge: an AI-native capability laboratory that turns difficult concepts into prediction-driven, manipulable worlds and small real-world missions, then fades assistance until the learner proves delayed, unaided transfer.

This report treats the supplied education research as a major evidence base, tests it against historical and current educational technology, and converts the synthesis into one product decision. It is intentionally narrower than a new school, broader than a tutor, and more demanding than a content application. The claim to prove is not that students enjoy Forge or complete more work. It is that, after using Forge, they can do something important without Forge that they could not do before.

1. Executive Decision

Build a capability laboratory, not another answer surface

The central failure of most AI-learning products is not that their explanations are poor. Frontier models can already produce competent explanations, examples, quizzes, study plans and conversational scaffolds. The deeper failure is that a high-quality assisted performance can look indistinguishable from learning while the learner's independent capability remains unchanged—or declines. In a controlled high-school mathematics experiment, unrestricted GPT assistance sharply improved practice performance but students later performed worse on an unaided examination; a deliberately guardrailed tutor mitigated the harm but did not create a positive exam advantage. Bastani et al., 2025 The OECD therefore frames the design problem as ensuring that generative AI becomes a learning partner rather than a shortcut. OECD, 2026

Forge is designed around the assisted-to-independent capability transition. Its primary interface is not an open chat. It is a bounded causal environment in which the learner must predict, manipulate, observe, explain, transfer and later return for a closed-tool proof. Conversation supports that loop but never becomes the loop.

Why this is the best tractable problem

The most consequential product-addressable problem is not lack of information. It is the failure to convert explanation, feedback and practice into a learner-owned model that survives the tool and transfers to a new context. This problem sits at the intersection of several common failures:

  1. learners often lack one or two hidden prerequisites but receive a full re-explanation of the current topic;
  2. abstract ideas remain verbal or symbolic instead of becoming manipulable and causally intelligible;
  3. assistance arrives before a committed attempt, so the system cannot diagnose the learner's actual model;
  4. practice uses near-identical items that create fluency without flexible transfer;
  5. products measure completion, immediate correctness or satisfaction rather than delayed unaided capability;
  6. projects and inquiry are introduced without enough foundation, while direct instruction often ends before meaningful application;
  7. AI makes polished output cheap, removing the natural evidence that thinking occurred.

Forge addresses these failures in one coherent loop. It does not attempt to solve poverty, school belonging, mental illness, family instability, peer relationships, safeguarding, teacher workload or every motivational problem. Those are partly or primarily human and institutional responsibilities. Forge should make teachers and parents more effective where present; it should not present itself as their replacement.

The missing primitive

Existing learning products help students receive explanations, complete practice or stay engaged, but they do not reliably convert assisted performance into independently transferable capability. The missing product primitive is a Capability Loop: a manipulable learning environment that measures and actively closes the gap between what a learner can do with AI and what the learner can later do alone.

The core product loop

Encounter a compelling anomaly
Commit a prediction or model
Manipulate a causal microworld
Receive the minimum useful intervention
Reconstruct the mechanism
Apply it in a contrast case
Test it in reality or create something
Return later for unaided transfer
Generate the next question

The sequence deliberately combines direct instruction, guided inquiry, retrieval, self-explanation, productive failure and construction. It does not turn any one of them into an ideology. Research on inquiry finds meaningful benefits when guidance is present; research on productive failure finds advantages for problem-solving before instruction under high-fidelity conditions, especially for older students and conceptual transfer; evidence on novice learning still warns against leaving beginners to search unbounded spaces. Lazonder & Harmsen, 2016 Sinha & Kapur, 2021

Immediate recommendation

Build a seven-day prototype around one misconception-rich physics idea—net force changes velocity; it is not required to maintain motion. Use one deterministic simulation, one hand-authored hint ladder, three transfer cases and one delayed closed-tool check. Do not build an open curriculum, a school dashboard, an AI avatar, a social feed or generative simulations. The first milestone is a measurable reduction in the assisted–unaided transfer gap, not a polished platform.

What must be proven

Forge deserves to become a company or enduring system only if it beats an excellent conventional explanation-and-practice condition on delayed unaided transfer, without requiring unsustainable content production or frustrating learners into abandonment. Until that is demonstrated, the larger vision is a hypothesis.

2. The Actual Problem Worth Solving

A hierarchy of learning problems

Educational weaknesses are not equally severe, equally tractable or equally appropriate for software. The hierarchy below prioritizes the combination of human importance, prevalence, causal leverage and product tractability.

Priority Problem Why it matters Software tractability What software cannot substitute for Product implication
1 Assisted performance without independent capability AI, worked solutions and answer tools can make the learner look competent while leaving no durable model High, if the product controls assistance and tests delayed transfer Honest assessment norms and human accountability Make the assisted–unaided gap the design target
2 Hidden prerequisite gaps A learner may fail a difficult problem because of graph reading, proportional reasoning, vocabulary or an earlier misconception High in bounded domains with curated concept graphs Comprehensive diagnosis of disability, language needs or life circumstances Probe the smallest plausible gap, then return to the original challenge
3 Abstract ideas without intuition Symbols are remembered procedurally but cannot be simulated mentally, compared or applied High where causal models can be visualized or manipulated Embodied expertise and real laboratory resources Build microworlds that expose causal structure, not decorative animation
4 Weak retrieval, spacing and variation Learners mistake familiarity for memory and repeat near-identical tasks High Curriculum time and consistent routines Schedule reconstruction and varied transfer after assistance
5 Poor feedback and error diagnosis Generic praise or answer revelation does not tell the learner what model failed Moderate to high with deterministic checks and constrained rubrics Expert judgment in ambiguous, high-stakes or creative work Diagnose error classes; give the minimum intervention
6 Low-quality questions and weak metacognition Learners ask for answers rather than mechanisms, tests, assumptions or boundaries Moderate; evidence is promising but thinner than retrieval or tutoring Intellectual culture, dialogue and mature exemplars Treat better questions as an output, not a decorative feature
7 No bridge from knowledge to reality Learning remains school-shaped and inert Moderate; product can propose, scaffold and record missions Safe spaces, tools, transport, adults and community access Every concept family should have a low-resource reality path
8 Avoidance of difficulty Learners either quit early or remain in comfortable fluency Moderate; challenge can be calibrated but motivation is contextual Belonging, identity, mental health, sleep and trusted adults Design productive struggle, never diagnose character from clicks

The single small intervention with disproportionate value

A learner's trajectory changes when they discover that difficult ideas are not mysterious verdicts on intelligence: they can expose their current model, test it, locate the gap, revise it and prove the revision. That experience builds earned confidence. The smallest high-leverage intervention is therefore not “personalized content.” It is a repeatable episode in which the learner experiences:

I predicted something, reality disagreed, I found the mechanism, and now I can use it somewhere new without help.

This episode improves knowledge, metacognition, willingness to attempt hard problems and the learner's model of what learning itself is. It also creates better evidence than a quiz completed with an invisible AI assistant.

Problems Forge should refuse to solve

Forge should not optimize away boredom in every moment; some repetition is required. It should not convert every interest into a credential. It should not simulate friendship, therapy or parental attachment. It should not infer emotional states from a camera. It should not replace outdoor play, craft, sport, live debate, laboratories or human mentorship with screen equivalents. It should not encourage a child to disclose intimate information so the model can feel “personal.”

These refusals are product decisions, not missing features. The FTC's actions against educational technology providers make clear that data minimization, educational-purpose limits and operator responsibility are not optional. FTC, 2023 UNICEF's current guidance similarly emphasizes child rights, privacy, fairness, transparency and accountability. UNICEF, 2025

Why “Unregretted Learning Time” is valuable but insufficient

“Maximize the learning minutes a student would later judge as deeply worth having spent” is an excellent ethical challenge to engagement-maximizing product culture. It forces the team to ask whether a session generated understanding, capability, a meaningful artifact or a better question. But it is not a sufficient north-star metric for four reasons:

  1. Retrospective satisfaction is noisy. A difficult session can feel bad before its value becomes visible; a frictionless explanation can feel excellent while producing weak retention.
  2. Learners cannot directly observe counterfactual learning. They do not know what would have happened with another method.
  3. Self-report can be gamed by product tone. Praise, narrative and social desirability can raise “worth it” ratings without changing capability.
  4. The metric can excuse paternalism. Designers may declare unpleasant time “good for you” without evidence.

Keep Unregretted Learning Time as a qualitative product principle and delayed experience measure. Use Delayed Unaided Transfer Gain per Active Learning Hour as the primary outcome. The two should agree over time: students should become more capable, and a day or week later they should recognize that the effort was worth it.

3. What the Attached Research Implies for Product Design

The report's strongest design constraints

The supplied report's central synthesis is not a single teaching method. It is a sequence: activate prior knowledge; explain or model when needed; guide practice; retrieve and space; provide feedback; vary contexts; require explanation and application; increase independence; revisit after delay; and eventually give real responsibility. That sequence implies eleven non-negotiable product rules.

Evidence-informed conclusion Product rule What to reject
Performance during assistance is not evidence of later learning Evaluate unseen, closed-tool transfer after delay Immediate correctness as the main success metric
Novices lack the schemas experts use Constrain the search space and expose prerequisites “Explore anything” interfaces for beginners
Retrieval and spacing improve durable access Require reconstruction after explanations and schedule delayed return Passive review and one-session mastery claims
Worked examples reduce unnecessary search Use examples when element interactivity is high, then fade steps Endless full solutions or examples with no subsequent generation
Guidance improves inquiry Let learners manipulate and ask, but build implicit and explicit constraints Unguided discovery as default
Productive failure can prepare older learners for instruction Elicit a committed model before teaching when the task is carefully bounded Random struggle, humiliation or “figure it out” pedagogy
Feedback is useful when actionable and timely Name the error class and point to the next cognitive action Generic praise, red crosses or answer reveal
Transfer requires varied contexts Move from near contrast to structurally similar but surface-different cases Repeated isomorphic drills alone
Projects work best with foundations and external standards Gate real-world missions on sufficient conceptual readiness Attractive artifacts with shallow understanding
Curiosity and autonomy support persistence Offer meaningful choices inside a coherent thread Infinite rabbit holes or manipulative novelty
Teachers, peers and mentors perform irreplaceable functions Escalate to humans and make evidence shareable Software pretending to be a complete educational institution

The sequence Forge should implement

Phase 1 — Expose the learner's current model

Start with an anomaly, comparison or consequential problem. Ask for a prediction, sketch, ranking, explanation or plan before assistance. The commitment creates diagnostic evidence and makes later feedback meaningful.

Phase 2 — Repair the minimum prerequisite

Use a short discriminating probe. If the problem is an earlier concept, branch for two to six minutes, then restore the original challenge. Never make the learner restart an entire course.

Phase 3 — Build intuition through constrained manipulation

Let the learner vary one or two causal quantities, compare representations and inspect counterexamples. The environment must make productive actions likely without scripting every move.

Phase 4 — Explain, model and fade

When search no longer produces useful information, provide a concise explanation or partial worked example. Ask the learner to reconstruct it, complete a missing step and state the mechanism in their own terms.

Phase 5 — Vary and transfer

Use a ladder: near contrast, new representation, surface-different case, then generative application. Assistance should decline across matched difficulty.

Phase 6 — Reality and responsibility

Propose a safe physical observation, calculation, build or decision. The learner produces evidence, not merely an answer.

Phase 7 — Delayed proof

After seven to fourteen days, remove the microworld and hints. Require explanation, prediction and a novel problem. Mastery is an evidence state, not a celebratory animation.

Phase 8 — Open the next question

Invite one question that changes a variable, tests a boundary or connects the mechanism. Preserve it in a question graph and offer a bounded next path.

What the product must preserve as unaided practice

The attached report correctly argues that the goal is not to simulate a world without tools. It is to maintain cognitive fallback and monitoring capacity. Within Forge, the following must recur without AI:

  • reading a compact technical explanation and identifying its claim;
  • drawing a model or diagram from memory;
  • estimation, proportional reasoning and basic algebra;
  • explaining a mechanism orally or in writing;
  • tracing code or equations when relevant;
  • making a prediction before a simulation;
  • observing and measuring a physical event;
  • solving an unseen transfer problem after delay;
  • deciding whether an AI explanation is wrong, incomplete or irrelevant.

The design inference is modest: ordinary learners should gain more access to notebooks, manipulable tools, good questions, mentors, peers, consequential projects and concentrated feedback. The product should not pretend to manufacture genius.

Biographical cross-check: learning lives, not genius folklore

A closer reading of primary and institutional accounts supports the report's design patterns, while also showing why biography cannot function as a controlled trial.

Michael Faraday — apprenticeship became experimental agency

Faraday's route combined a bookbinder's apprenticeship, voracious reading, attendance at scientific lectures, correspondence, laboratory assistantship under Humphry Davy, public explanation and thousands of experiments. The Royal Institution records his progression from laboratory assistant to director and professor; its correspondence archive documents his scientific learning, networks and rise through real work rather than a clean sequence of courses. Royal Institution, Faraday biography Royal Institution, Faraday correspondence

Transferable condition: access to tools, a prepared laboratory, expert observation, notebooks, repeated experiments and a responsibility to explain publicly. Non-transferable advantage: entry into one of the world's rare scientific institutions and an exceptional lifetime of concentration. Forge can reproduce manipulability, prediction and explanation; it cannot simulate institutional belonging or expert trust.

Srinivasa Ramanujan — self-direction needed a bridge to disciplinary standards

Ramanujan's notebooks and largely self-directed mathematical exploration demonstrate extraordinary generative power. They also show the limits of romanticizing isolation: unconventional notation, missing proofs, uneven exposure and the decisive importance of correspondence and collaboration with G. H. Hardy. MacTutor History of Mathematics, Ramanujan

Transferable condition: sustained problem generation, notebook use, pattern seeking and intellectual obsession. Design warning: a product should not mistake prolific conjecture for validated knowledge. Learner questions and constructions need connection to shared definitions, proof standards, counterexamples and expert communities.

Marie Curie — formal foundation, laboratory endurance and collaborative instrumentation

Curie's path included exceptional formal study in mathematics and physics after restricted educational opportunity in Poland, followed by laboratory partnership and sustained measurement work. Nobel sources record her Sorbonne education and collaborative laboratory research with Pierre Curie. Nobel Prize, Marie Curie Q&A

Transferable condition: deep disciplinary foundation plus patient empirical work and instrument-mediated observation. Design warning: “follow curiosity” is insufficient; technical competence and rigorous measurement make curiosity scientifically productive. Forge should let intuition open the question but make evidence and representation close the claim.

Katherine Johnson — mentors, questions and consequential verification

NASA's biographical materials describe both Johnson's early mathematical fascination and the role of professors who deliberately prepared her for research mathematics. At NASA, she asked questions, entered technical meetings, worked from geometry when the field lacked established texts and performed calculations with direct mission consequence. NASA Science, Katherine Johnson NASA, A Lifetime of STEM

Transferable condition: a mentor who raises the standard, access to serious work, permission to ask clarifying questions, verification and accountability. Non-transferable context: unusual precocity, family sacrifice, historical segregation and a unique institutional moment. Forge should prepare learners to use mentors well and treat verification as real work—not suggest that software replaces either.

Tu Youyou — cross-tradition synthesis under a real public-health problem

Tu's own Nobel biographical account describes rigorous pharmaceutical training, hands-on extraction methods, additional study of traditional Chinese medicine, review of thousands of prescriptions, repeated failed experiments and a return to an ancient extraction clue that changed the experimental method. Nobel Prize, Tu Youyou biographical account

Transferable condition: cross-disciplinary source search, respect for inherited knowledge without suspending empirical test, problem-driven persistence and revisiting assumptions after inconsistent results. Design warning: heroic retellings can obscure large teams, dangerous conditions and political context. Forge can teach “return to the source and alter the mechanism,” but must never imitate unsafe self-experimentation.

Grace Hopper — theory met machines, debugging and institutional teams

The Smithsonian's account shows Hopper moving from advanced mathematics and teaching into wartime work on the Mark I, where mathematical knowledge met an unprecedented machine and a team with real operating constraints. Smithsonian, Grace Hopper

Transferable condition: trace systems, inspect errors, build abstractions that reduce human friction and explain technical ideas across roles. The popular “first bug” story is often simplified; the useful pattern is not a slogan about failure but sustained debugging in a real computational environment. Forge's code and systems labs should expose state and require diagnosis rather than regenerate solutions.

Octavia Butler — deliberate writing, research collections and revision

The Huntington archive contains hundreds of boxes of Butler's manuscripts, drafts, notebooks, notecards, diaries, commonplace books and research materials. The physical record contradicts the myth that creative achievement emerges mainly from spontaneous inspiration. Huntington Library, Octavia E. Butler papers

Transferable condition: capture ideas, research beyond one's immediate experience, draft repeatedly and maintain long projects through ordinary sessions. Design warning: productivity instrumentation can colonize the private space that made such work possible. A Forge notebook should support revision and provenance while allowing unscored, private and exportable thought.

Mae Jemison — breadth was integrated through action, not sampled as content

NASA documents Jemison's education in chemical engineering and African and Afro-American studies, medical training, Peace Corps service, astronaut research and later work integrating technology, culture and development. NASA, Mae Jemison

Transferable condition: multiple disciplines become powerful when joined around real problems and responsibilities. Design warning: interdisciplinary education is not a carousel of themed examples; learners need enough depth in each contributing field to make the synthesis honest.

Wangari Maathai — observation became community-scale action

Maathai connected biological training with observed deforestation, rural women's articulated needs, practical tree planting, organization and political struggle. Her Nobel lecture explicitly links childhood observation, formal education and local community need. Nobel Prize, Wangari Maathai lecture

Transferable condition: notice a local system, listen to affected people, act at manageable scale, observe consequences and connect technical work to civic responsibility. Design warning: software cannot manufacture community legitimacy. Field missions should culminate in respectful observation or contribution, not gamified “impact.”

What can reasonably be democratized

Across these lives, the generalizable unit is not personality. It is access to a set of learning conditions:

  • a notebook that preserves questions, models and revisions;
  • tools that let ideas meet consequences;
  • mentors who raise standards and open institutions;
  • peers and correspondence that expose other ways of thinking;
  • a real problem or audience that makes quality consequential;
  • enough foundational knowledge to make exploration productive;
  • sustained time and repeated return;
  • permission to cross domains without abandoning rigor;
  • error records and counterexamples rather than polished final answers only;
  • increasing responsibility as capability becomes credible.

Forge can make several of these conditions cheaper and more available. It cannot equalize family resources, remove structural discrimination, supply every laboratory, create human friendship or guarantee obsession. Its proper ambition is to make rare learning affordances more common—not to automate exceptional people.

4. History of Previous Attempts

The long arc: education technology repeatedly rediscovers the same partial truths

The history of educational invention is not a sequence of foolish failures followed by modern intelligence. Each wave solved a real constraint and exposed a new one. Socratic dialogue emphasized reasons, apprenticeship connected knowledge to work, Montessori designed a prepared environment, mastery learning varied time, programmed instruction demanded active responses, constructionism made computers objects to think with, intelligent tutors modeled steps, simulations made invisible systems manipulable, MOOCs distributed lectures, adaptive systems sequenced content, and current language models make feedback abundant. The missing integration is a loop that combines these strengths while measuring independence.

Attempt or tradition Core loop What it got right Where it failed or remained incomplete What Forge preserves

The strongest historical synthesis

Three traditions should be combined rather than chosen:

  1. Cognitive apprenticeship: model, coach, scaffold, articulate, reflect, explore and fade.
  2. Constructionist microworlds: let learners manipulate and build objects that reveal their thinking.
  3. Mastery with retrieval and transfer: vary time and support, but require later evidence under reduced assistance.

AI newly makes this integration plausible because it can interpret open explanations, generate contrast cases, translate representations, maintain longitudinal evidence and vary hints in real time. What it still cannot safely do alone is guarantee truth, infer a child's internal state, create high-fidelity simulations on demand, replace human judgment or establish that a learner can perform independently. Those functions require deterministic systems, curated content, careful evaluation and humans.

Why educational technology underperforms even when the idea is good

A product can contain an evidence-based technique and still fail. Retrieval can become stressful grading. Mastery can become endless remediation. projects can become inequitable homework. Simulations can become unguided clicking. Personalization can lower expectations. Tutoring can become answer delivery. The unit of analysis must therefore be the implemented learning loop, including learner incentives, teacher burden, measurement, timing and the surrounding institution—not the presence of a named technique.

The OLPC results are especially instructive: the programme successfully changed access and computer skill while failing to change academic outcomes, partly because classroom practice and teacher digital capability changed little. Cueto et al., 2025 The lesson is not “technology never works.” Intelligent tutoring systems and human tutoring show that well-specified feedback loops can generate substantial gains, while implementation and evaluation alignment strongly moderate results. Kulik & Fletcher, 2016 Nickow, Oreopoulos & Quan, 2020

6. Future Learner and Future Capability Map

What becomes abundant, and what remains scarce

Between 2026 and 2035, capable multimodal models will make competent-looking explanation, translation, coding, summarization, illustration, example generation and routine feedback increasingly abundant. Labour-market evidence does not support a simple forecast of wholesale job replacement: the ILO estimates that one in four jobs has some generative-AI exposure, while only a much smaller share sits in the highest exposure category, and it judges transformation more likely than complete automation. Capabilities at risk of atrophy

The primary danger is not that learners forget facts because search exists. It is that they stop performing the cognitive operations that create organized knowledge and self-regulation:

  • framing a problem before seeking solutions;
  • holding a multi-step argument in mind;
  • retrieving a model without cues;
  • writing to discover what one thinks;
  • estimating before calculating;
  • tracing and debugging rather than regenerating;
  • tolerating uncertainty long enough to investigate;
  • observing the world rather than accepting a representation;
  • distinguishing “I recognize this” from “I can produce and use this”;
  • persisting after a first failed attempt;
  • making a judgment for which one remains accountable.

Forge should treat these operations as protected exercise. AI is allowed to make the exercise better targeted, more varied and more feedback-rich; it is not allowed to remove the operation.

7. The Missing Product Primitive

From tutor to Capability Loop

Most current categories are organized around a delivery object: course, tutor, quiz, note, flashcard, video, learning management system or chatbot. Forge is organized around a state transition in the learner.

Current model
often tacit or wrong
→
Evidence-producing attempt
prediction, sketch, plan
→
Revised causal model
built through contrast and feedback
→
Independent transfer
new context, no tool

The primitive has five required properties:

  1. A committed pre-assistance state. The system must know what the learner thought before it helped.
  2. A world with consequences. Some claims must be tested by deterministic simulation, external source or physical observation rather than language-model agreement.
  3. A bounded support policy. Help is staged and costly in the learning model; it cannot silently become full solution generation.
  4. A transfer contract. Every assisted concept leads to a novel unaided demonstration after support fades.
  5. A longitudinal evidence trail. The system stores what the learner did, what assistance was required, what transferred and which questions emerged—not a reductive personality score.

Why an AI tutor is not enough

A tutor can ask good questions, but open conversation creates five structural problems. The learner can request or indirectly extract an answer; the model's diagnosis is based mainly on language; the environment cannot guarantee causal correctness; the session usually ends when the immediate problem is solved; and the product has little reason to bring the learner back for an unaided transfer check. Study modes and Khanmigo reduce some of these risks, and Khan Academy's decision to disable Khanmigo on quizzes and mastery challenges is an important precedent. Khan Academy, updated 2024 Forge makes the boundary the architecture rather than a setting.

Why a simulation library is not enough

High-quality simulations can create “engaged exploration” when they offer concrete real-world links, representations unavailable in the physical world, productive constraints and dynamic feedback. Podolefsky, Perkins & Adams, 2010 Yet a learner can click without predicting, leave a misconception intact, fail to connect representations or enjoy a simulation without transferring. Forge wraps each microworld in a learner-model elicitation, support policy, explanation reconstruction, reality task and delayed proof.

Why this primitive is newly viable

Previous systems could provide one or two components. Current AI makes the full loop affordable enough to attempt:

  • multimodal models can interpret drawings, spoken explanations and photographed setups;
  • language models can classify misconception evidence and phrase the next hint in the learner's language;
  • generative systems can create surface-varied transfer cases around a validated underlying model;
  • persistent state can schedule delayed retrieval and track assistance fading;
  • code-generation systems can accelerate authoring of parameterized worlds, although expert validation remains mandatory;
  • voice can make the interface feel like thinking aloud without making conversation the product;
  • source-grounded retrieval can keep explanations tied to vetted materials;
  • low-cost phones contain cameras, microphones, accelerometers and timers that can connect models to reality.

The key invention is not any one capability. It is the policy that decides what remains the learner's work.

One-sentence description. Forge turns a difficult idea into a deterministic world the learner can manipulate, requires a prediction before assistance, and withholds completion until the learner later demonstrates the principle in an unseen context without AI.

8.2 Counterfactual Foundry — learn by changing the world

One-sentence description. The learner receives a compact model of a historical, scientific, economic or civic situation, predicts what follows if one assumption changes, and iteratively improves the model by comparing counterfactuals with evidence.

Design question Answer
Target learner and first use case Ages 15–19 learning economics, history or environmental science; first wedge: causal reasoning about inflation, incentives, epidemics or energy systems.
Core learning loop State causal story → commit forecast → change one variable or rule → inspect simulated or documented consequences → compare rival models → revise → defend a decision.
Why the learner returns A branching “what if?” investigation produces meaningful suspense: the learner wants to know whether the world behaves as predicted.
Cognition the learner owns Causal assumptions, prediction, selection of decisive evidence, trade-off judgment and final written or oral defence.
What AI contributes Surfaces missing variables, generates rival hypotheses, retrieves evidence, runs bounded agent-based scenarios and challenges overconfident causal claims.
Durable knowledge mechanism Generative prediction, contrasting cases, explanation, analogical transfer and repeated use of the same causal schema across different domains.
Curiosity mechanism Counterfactuals convert static facts into live alternatives while source constraints prevent unconstrained historical fantasy.

Practical application Learners write policy memos, allocate budgets, design experiments or compare actual outcomes with model forecasts.
Primary risk Simulations can falsely imply precision, embed contested assumptions and reward confident storytelling over historical discipline.
Why it was not already solved Case methods are expensive to facilitate; educational simulations are usually fixed and shallow; generic AI counterfactuals often hallucinate or obscure assumptions.
Defensibility A library of audited causal models, source-linked scenario rules, disagreement maps and evaluation tasks becomes more valuable than fluent scenario generation alone.
MVP Three authored scenarios with transparent causal diagrams and deterministic sensitivity analysis, plus an AI cross-examiner.
Long-term potential A cross-domain judgment laboratory for policy, business, science and civic decision-making.
Evidence confidence Moderate Strong reasoning rationale; difficult validity and authoring problem.

10. Top-Five Pre-Mortems and Red Teams

10.1 Forge Labs

One-year pre-mortem

Forge launched with beautiful simulations and earned enthusiastic demo reactions. After twelve months, students had tried two labs but rarely returned. The authoring team could not ship reliable concepts quickly enough; generated variations contained subtle errors; the hint governor felt stubborn; and delayed proof notifications were ignored. Immediate post-tests looked positive, but gains over PhET plus a good worksheet disappeared at seven days. Parents saw another screen, teachers saw another content library, and a general chatbot copied the conversational portions.

Student red team

Students will try to drag sliders randomly until the target state appears, ask the side chat for the answer, open a second AI window, intentionally understate knowledge to receive easier tasks, memorize interface patterns, abandon delayed checks and optimize for the visible capability badge. Some will find prediction irritating: “Let me play.” Others will find the reality mission childish or inconvenient. Advanced learners may resent being forced through steps they can already perform.

Parent and teacher red team

Adults may distrust unproven AI diagnosis, worry about screen time and data, dislike a product that does not map visibly to the curriculum, or see physical missions as homework supervision transferred to families. Teachers may fear another dashboard, a conflict with their terminology, or remediation that labels students incorrectly. Physics educators may reject simulations that hide measurement uncertainty or present idealized worlds as reality.

Learning-science red team

A learner can manipulate without explaining, explain with verbal fluency but no model, succeed on near-transfer items generated from a common template, or forget because the delayed check is too brief. Productive struggle can become unproductive withholding. Microworlds can reduce cognitive load so much that the learner fails when notation returns. The system can overfit to diagnosed misconceptions and miss a different underlying gap.

Product and commoditization red team

Frontier-model providers can generate interactive SVGs, code sandboxes and Socratic prompts. PhET already has trusted simulations; Khan Academy has content, distribution and mastery infrastructure; Brilliant has interactive pedagogy. Forge could become a feature: “study mode with a simulation.”

Equity red team

Fast devices, quiet space and English fluency may improve the experience. Physical missions can presume household materials or safe outdoor access. Learners with motor, visual, reading or executive-function differences may be penalized by interaction traces. A family with a science-literate adult can extend the lab; another cannot.

Revision after attack

  • Make the committed prediction and delayed unaided transfer task the proprietary core, not the simulation or chat.
  • Benchmark against PhET plus expert-authored guidance, not a weak control.
  • Use deterministic simulations and pre-validated transfer families in the MVP; prohibit unrestricted model-generated physics.
  • Give competent learners a fast diagnostic bypass and make support optional only after evidence.
  • Offer keyboard, screen-reader, high-contrast and low-bandwidth representations; provide equivalent no-material reality tasks.
  • Keep the first session under 18 minutes and make the delayed proof a two-minute “surprise return,” not homework.
  • Show adults evidence of what the learner can now do unaided, not a time-on-app dashboard.

Revised verdict: still first. The attack narrows the product and strengthens the evidence requirement.

10.2 Counterfactual Foundry

One-year pre-mortem

Students loved asking “what if?” but the product became a narrative toy. Agent-based outputs looked authoritative despite fragile assumptions; content review was expensive; teachers rejected controversial models; and learners acquired confidence in simulation stories rather than causal humility. There was no reliable ground truth for many scenarios, so evaluation devolved into rubric-scored prose.

Student red team

Learners will choose amusing extreme scenarios, change many variables at once, cherry-pick outputs, treat the model as an oracle, or ask AI to write the policy memo. They may avoid learning baseline facts because the simulated world appears self-explanatory.

Parent and teacher red team

Adults may see ideological bias in historical or policy assumptions. Teachers may lack time to inspect model rules. Parents may object to political simulations or believe the tool teaches relativism. Administrators will ask how a counterfactual maps to examinable content.

Learning-science red team

Novices cannot reason productively with a model they do not understand. Open counterfactual exploration can impose excessive search load. The system may reward coherent explanations unsupported by evidence. Near transfer among scenarios is easier than real-world causal inference.

Product and commoditization red team

Generic models are exceptionally good at generating plausible counterfactual prose. Without transparent, validated causal models, the product is merely a themed chatbot. With them, authoring becomes a research institute rather than a startup.

Equity red team

Learners with stronger background knowledge, debate experience and language fluency gain more. Cultural assumptions embedded in scenarios can disadvantage others; disputed contexts may be presented through dominant-source archives.

Revision after attack

Start only in domains with explicit, inspectable and computable models—simple ecology, mechanics, probability and introductory economics—not open historical causation. Display assumptions and uncertainty at all times. Require baseline reconstruction and one-variable-at-a-time sensitivity before free exploration. Treat the model as a claim to audit, not the truth.

Revised verdict: an excellent later Forge mode, not the first company. It becomes stronger when anchored in the same capability-loop architecture.

10.3 Fieldcraft Missions

One-year pre-mortem

The first missions produced beautiful stories, but completion was low. Parents did not want children sent outside by an app; schools required risk assessments; phone sensors produced inconsistent data; urban, rural and disabled learners had different affordances; and quality varied with family support. The team spent more effort on safety and logistics than learning.

Student red team

Students can fabricate measurements, upload old photographs, let a parent complete the task, choose trivial observations or skip the abstract model. Some will experience fieldwork as imposed chores. Others will wander into unsafe locations or film people without consent.

Parent and teacher red team

Adults may reject location use, cameras, outdoor instructions and implied supervision. Teachers may face material procurement, weather and class-management problems. The product can look like an activity generator rather than rigorous instruction.

Learning-science red team

Concrete experiences do not automatically produce abstraction. Learners may remember “the rug experiment” without understanding net force. Measurement noise can obscure the intended concept. Too much procedural work can consume the learning minute.

Product and commoditization red team

Any multimodal model can suggest household experiments. The hard asset is safe, validated protocol and concept integration, which is expensive and local. As a standalone app, frequency may be low.

Equity red team

Local safety, parental time, mobility, tools, housing and public space are unequally distributed. Sensor quality and data cost vary. Requiring photographs can expose homes and locations.

Revision after attack

Make missions optional reality bridges after a digital lab, not the full first product. Never require location storage, faces or identifiable home images. Provide seated, indoor, no-material equivalents. Use manually validated protocols with a safety classification and adult co-use only when necessary. Accept honest “not possible here” without penalty.

Revised verdict: retain as Forge's third act. It is a powerful differentiator only after the conceptual loop works digitally.

10.4 Question Atlas

One-year pre-mortem

Early adopters produced impressive maps, but most students opened with “I don't know what to ask.” Maps became cluttered knowledge-management artifacts, AI generated sophisticated-looking questions the learner did not own, and teachers struggled to evaluate progress. Strong, motivated learners benefited; the students most in need of structure churned.

Student red team

Learners will accept generated questions, accumulate branches, label trivial curiosity as progress, avoid answering anything, or use endless exploration to escape disciplined practice. They can game question-quality indicators with formulaic phrases such as “what evidence would falsify…?”

Parent and teacher red team

Adults may see meandering without curriculum coverage. Teachers cannot review hundreds of personalized branches. Parents may worry about rabbit holes, inappropriate sources or a lack of visible mastery.

Learning-science red team

Questioning without knowledge produces shallow or unanswerable inquiry. The tool can privilege verbal sophistication, confuse curiosity with learning and weaken shared knowledge. A question rubric can make authentic curiosity performative.

Product and commoditization red team

Graph views and question generation are easy to copy. Note-taking and research products can add them. The value depends on longitudinal question quality and investigation outcomes, which take time to prove.

Equity red team

Students with prior cultural capital, broad reading and adult guidance generate richer paths. Learners writing in a second language or with dyslexia may be underrated despite strong thinking.

Revision after attack

Embed a small question trail inside a bounded capability lab. Questions earn continuation when they clarify a mechanism, propose a discriminating test, reveal a boundary or enable an action. AI may offer transformations but never populate the graph as if they were learner-generated. Language polish is scored separately from epistemic substance.

Revised verdict: not the first product; its question ladder becomes a supporting mechanism inside Forge.

10.5 Cognitive Firewall

One-year pre-mortem

The product correctly identified cognitive outsourcing, but students turned it off. Schools adopted it as an academic-integrity monitor, creating distrust. General AI providers added a “learning protection” toggle. Because the firewall lacked an intrinsically rewarding learning environment, it felt like friction imposed on an easier tool.

Student red team

Use a second device, paste a disguised request, claim production mode, ask for “examples” that reveal the solution, or intentionally fail the first attempt. Learners can resent a system that decides what their mind should do.

Parent and teacher red team

Some adults will demand surveillance and block lists; others will reject paternalism. Teachers may not agree on which operations must remain unaided. The browser extension may break workflows or expose private content.

Learning-science red team

Withholding answers is not teaching. A learner with a prerequisite gap may be forced into pointless struggle. Assistance accounting can reward low help rather than high learning. “AI-free” work can become a purity ritual rather than an evidence-based choice.

Product and commoditization red team

The policy layer is copyable by model platforms and operating systems. Distribution depends on access to other products, browsers or school mandates. It is more likely a feature or standard than a standalone destination.

Equity red team

Students who can afford private tutors or alternate tools bypass restrictions; students governed by schools experience stricter limits. Disabled learners may rely on assistance that superficially resembles outsourcing.

Revision after attack

Do not sell restriction. Build the governor inside an environment learners choose because the simulation and insight are valuable. Make modes explicit and learner-controlled within safety bounds. Measure transfer rather than low help. Treat accessibility support as cognitive access, not dependency. Publish a portable assistance taxonomy so the policy can become a trusted standard even if the standalone firewall does not.

Revised verdict: indispensable as Forge's constitutional layer, weak as the primary product.

What the red teams changed

The strongest concepts do combine—but only in a strict hierarchy:

  1. Forge's Capability Loop is the product.
  2. Cognitive Firewall becomes the dependency governor inside that loop.
  3. Question Atlas becomes the question-development trail, not an open map at launch.
  4. Fieldcraft becomes an optional reality mission after conceptual reconstruction.
  5. Counterfactual Foundry becomes a future advanced world-model mode once deterministic STEM labs validate the architecture.

This synthesis adds no independent feed, curriculum generator, mentor marketplace or social network. Each retained mechanism strengthens the transition from assisted experience to independent capability.

11. Final Product Selection

Final decision

The best thing to build is Forge: an AI-native capability laboratory that turns difficult concepts into prediction-driven, manipulable worlds and small real-world missions, then fades assistance until the learner proves delayed, unaided transfer.

Why this is the most important tractable problem

The largest education problems—poverty, school capacity, teacher supply, family instability, curriculum coherence and unequal opportunity—cannot be solved by one software product. The product-tractable problem with unusually high leverage is narrower:

Students can increasingly obtain competent explanations and completed outputs, yet neither schools nor AI products reliably ensure that those experiences become knowledge and judgment the learner can later use alone.

This gap existed before generative AI. AI makes it larger and more visible because assistance is now fluent, immediate and nearly unlimited. In the Bastani et al. high-school mathematics field experiment, unrestricted GPT support markedly improved practice performance yet reduced later unaided performance, while a guardrailed tutoring design mitigated the damage. Bastani et al., 2025 Forge makes this gap the primary product object.

Why now

Four capabilities have crossed a useful threshold at the same time:

  • multimodal models can interpret rough explanations, drawings, speech and images well enough to support—though not autonomously certify—diagnosis;
  • frontier models can generate context-sensitive hints and contrast cases rather than one fixed feedback string;
  • deterministic browser simulations can be built and deployed cheaply, and code agents reduce—but do not remove—the authoring cost;
  • persistent scheduling and instrumentation can measure assistance, delayed retrieval and transfer at individual and cohort level.

The timing is also defensive. Study Mode, Khanmigo and similar products show that the major platforms recognize the answer-machine problem. OpenAI explicitly notes that Study Mode can still provide direct answers and make mistakes; Khan Academy disables Khanmigo in quizzes and mastery challenges. OpenAI, Study Mode FAQ, updated 2026 Khan Academy, support documentation The opportunity is not to repeat their conversation layer. It is to build a new primary object around consequence and transfer.

What must be true

Forge is worth building only if five claims survive testing:

  1. Prediction-plus-manipulation-plus-reconstruction produces greater delayed transfer than an excellent explanation and guided worksheet at comparable time.
  2. Learners voluntarily tolerate answer withholding when the underlying environment creates a fast and meaningful insight.
  3. A bounded model can diagnose enough common misconceptions to choose a better next move than a fixed script without introducing unacceptable errors.
  4. The central loop can be authored in weeks per concept family—not months per lesson—while preserving scientific validity.
  5. Assistance can measurably fade across matched challenge without simply making tasks easier.

These are falsifiable product hypotheses, not branding assumptions.

Why not build Gapfinder first?

Gapfinder has the easiest commercial story: diagnose hidden prerequisites and repair them. It is also closest to existing mastery and tutoring systems. It solves confusion but not the larger product opportunity: enabling the learner to form intuition, ask better questions, touch reality and become less dependent on AI. Gapfinder is a necessary subsystem of Forge's productive-struggle engine.

Why not build Cognitive Firewall first?

The answer-machine risk is urgent, but people rarely adopt a product whose main benefit is restraint. The best dependency governor is embedded in an environment worth using for its own sake. Forge makes constrained assistance feel like part of solving a mystery rather than a rule imposed from outside.

Why not build Fieldcraft first?

Fieldcraft is more embodied and may better protect childhood from screen capture, but it has higher safety, supervision, accessibility and measurement burdens. It also needs a reliable bridge from experience to abstraction. Forge can add a small reality mission after a validated digital model, gaining much of the value without making logistics the first existential risk.

Why not build an apprenticeship network?

Human mentorship and real responsibility may be the highest-value long-term layer. They are also supply-constrained, legally sensitive and operationally complex. A small team should first build a digital loop that can demonstrate capability and prepare learners to use scarce mentor time well. Forge's evidence trail can later make mentor matching more credible.

Why not simply make the best AI tutor?

“Best tutor” is a moving target owned by foundation-model companies, curriculum platforms and well-funded incumbents. It is also underspecified educationally. A more eloquent response can increase dependence. Forge can use whichever model currently gives the best diagnosis while preserving a product constitution—committed prediction, deterministic consequence, bounded support, reconstruction, transfer and delayed proof—that a generic conversation does not naturally enforce.

13. Complete Product Blueprint

13.1 Product thesis

Learner problem

A student can recognize a procedure, follow an explanation or complete a problem with AI, yet still lacks a causal model that can be retrieved and transferred when the wording, representation or tool access changes.

Missing primitive

A governed Capability Loop that records the learner's pre-help model, produces consequences in a validated environment, limits assistance, and closes only after delayed unaided transfer.

Intervention

Short, prediction-driven laboratories that combine deterministic simulation, diagnostic contrasts, a graduated hint policy, explanation reconstruction, a reality mission and a future proof run.

Expected transformation

The learner moves from “I followed it when it was shown” to “I can explain, use, test and recognize this principle in a different situation without the system.”

The immediate promise to the learner is not “learn anything” or “get better grades.” It is:

Give Forge fifteen focused minutes on one idea that keeps confusing you. Leave able to predict something you could not predict before—and prove it later without us.

The first concept families are:

  1. velocity versus acceleration;
  2. net force versus direction of motion;
  3. mass, force and acceleration;
  4. friction and idealization;

  6. energy transfer and conservation;
  7. momentum, impulse and collision.

13.3 Product constitution

Every lab must satisfy eight rules. A lesson that violates one is content, not Forge.

  1. The learner acts before the model explains. A prediction, sketch, ranking, estimate or verbal model must be committed.
  2. The environment can disagree. Core consequences come from deterministic code, validated data or physical observation—not language-model approval.
  3. Help is staged. The system first probes, then cues a relevant distinction, then changes representation, then offers a partial example. Full exposition is a last resort followed by reconstruction.
  4. Assistance is visible. Learner and system know what support was used; help is not moralized, but it cannot masquerade as independent performance.
  5. Understanding crosses representations. Words, diagrams, graphs, equations, code and physical examples are connected deliberately.
  6. A new context is mandatory. Repeating the same interface state does not count as transfer.
  7. Mastery expires until retrieved. A successful session creates a future proof run rather than a permanent badge.
  8. The system becomes quieter. As evidence improves, hints fade, explanatory turns shorten and the learner controls more of the investigation.

13.4 The capability object

Forge should not represent a learner as a single level or personality profile. The atomic object is a capability claim:

Under specified conditions, the learner can perform a meaningful cognitive action, in one or more representations, with a measured level of assistance, and can transfer it after a defined delay.

13.5 Knowledge and mastery model

The initial concept graph is expert-authored and intentionally small. Nodes include concepts, operations and common misconception families; edges include prerequisite, contrast, representation and application relationships.

State dimensions

For each capability, Forge maintains separate estimates for:

  • concept availability: can the learner retrieve the principle without cues?
  • procedural execution: can the learner carry out a relevant method?
  • representation coordination: can the learner connect diagram, graph, equation and language?
  • misconception competition: does a seductive incorrect model reappear under pressure?
  • transfer breadth: how different can a context become before performance fails?
  • retrieval strength: what happens after delay?
  • assistance profile: which kinds of support restore progress?
  • epistemic stance: can the learner state uncertainty and identify what evidence would decide?

These are not averaged into one mastery percentage. The learner sees a concise evidence statement: “You can explain this in words and diagrams after one hint; the graph version and seven-day proof are still open.”

Updating the model

The MVP should use interpretable Bayesian or logistic updates over hand-calibrated evidence, not an opaque neural learner model. An event updates only the dimensions it genuinely tests. A correct multiple-choice prediction with low discrimination should move confidence little. A novel explanation plus a successful transfer should move it more. A failure can decrease certainty or increase uncertainty, but it does not automatically prove a prerequisite deficit.

The model should support three outputs:

  1. next-move hypotheses, such as “test whether the learner confuses velocity and acceleration”;
  2. support decisions, such as “change representation before giving a worked step”;
  3. evidence summaries, such as “independent transfer observed twice across two contexts, last retrieved 12 days ago.”

13.6 Personalization without intellectual isolation

Forge personalizes:

  • entry point and diagnostic length;
  • surface context and examples;
  • wording and language support;
  • hint timing and representation;
  • pacing, accessibility and branch choice;
  • which misconception test occurs next;
  • which one of several equivalent reality missions is feasible.

Forge does not personalize away:

  • the central capability standard;
  • exposure to shared disciplinary knowledge;
  • the need to explain and retrieve;
  • the existence of unfamiliar contexts;
  • evidence quality;
  • cumulative closed-tool checks;
  • the learner's right to know what the system inferred.

14.3 The support state machine

The support policy should be deterministic at the top level even when AI phrases the interaction. A simplified state machine:

OBSERVE → ELICIT → PROBE → EXPLORE → {WAIT | CUE | REPRESENT | EXAMPLE | REPAIR} → RECONSTRUCT → CLOSED_TRANSFER → REALITY → DELAYED_PROOF
  • WAIT: learner has a plausible plan and is making informative actions.
  • CUE: direct attention to one relevant feature without specifying the operation.
  • REPRESENT: switch from words to diagram, graph, animation, analogy or manipulable case.
  • EXAMPLE: show only the smallest worked substep or analogous case needed to restart.
  • REPAIR: branch to a two-to-five-minute prerequisite micro-loop, then return to the original phenomenon.

The state machine cannot be bypassed by a language-model impulse to be helpful. A model may recommend a transition with confidence and rationale; a policy service accepts, rejects or escalates based on allowed actions.

14.4 Failure is evidence, not punishment

A wrong prediction should create a fast, emotionally safe discrepancy. The interface says, in effect, “Good—now we know which model to test,” not “incorrect.” A successful guess with no explanation should not be celebrated as mastery. The product rewards honest commitment because hiding one's uncertainty reduces the quality of the experiment.

The system should distinguish:

  • prediction error: useful evidence about the model;
  • execution error: calculation, reading or interface slip;
  • representation error: understands verbally but not graphically, or vice versa;
  • prerequisite gap: lacks a necessary concept or operation;
  • attention lapse: inconsistent with surrounding evidence;
  • model contradiction: explanation conflicts with observed result;
  • epistemic overconfidence: high confidence despite weak basis.

Only repeated, discriminating evidence should create a misconception hypothesis.

14.5 Why the loop should work—and where evidence stops

The loop combines evidence-backed components: retrieval practice supports retention; spacing improves long-term memory; worked examples and fading help novices manage cognitive load; guidance improves inquiry outcomes; productive failure can outperform instruction-first designs when exploration is well designed and followed by consolidation; and active learning outperforms lecture alone in many STEM settings. Agarwal et al., 2021 Cepeda et al., 2006 Sinha & Kapur, 2021 Lazonder & Harmsen, 2016 Freeman et al., 2014

No cited study validates the exact Forge loop as a package. Sequencing, friction, domain dependence and implementation quality can reverse benefits. The first product obligation is therefore experimental, not rhetorical.

15. Question, Curiosity, Intuition, and Productive-Struggle Systems

15.1 Question-development system: from request to investigation

Questions are stored in the learner's Trail, but the visible experience should not feel like a rubric. The learner sees one natural coaching move: “Can we make that question answerable?” or “What result would separate those two explanations?” Internally, Forge uses the following ladder.

Stage Typical question Upgrade move Evidence of improvement
Notice “What is happening?” Name the specific change, object or pattern Refers to observable features rather than a broad topic
Clarify “What does acceleration mean?” Distinguish related terms and representations Identifies ambiguity or contrast
Mechanism “What could make the velocity change?” Name a causal relation, not merely a label Proposes a mechanism with variables
Prediction “If friction is zero, what happens next?” Commit direction, magnitude or ranking before observing Produces a falsifiable expectation
Discrimination “What test would separate constant force from constant velocity?” Change one variable or identify decisive evidence Test gives different predictions under rival models
Boundary “Where would this model stop working?” Find idealization, scale, assumption or counterexample States conditions under which the claim is limited
Connection “Is changing direction without changing speed also acceleration?” Map the structure to another domain or representation Preserves causal relation across contexts
Design/decision “How could I build a fair test?” Specify constraint, metric and trade-off Question enables an action or choice

Internal quality dimensions

Forge estimates five dimensions separately:

  1. specificity — are entities and variables clear?
  2. causal depth — does the question seek mechanism rather than label?
  3. testability — could evidence change the answer?
  4. assumption awareness — are boundary conditions visible?
  5. generativity — would an answer enable another useful prediction, action or question?

Language sophistication is not a quality dimension. “What changes if I double the push but keep the mass the same?” can be excellent even if grammatically simple. The product never awards points for inserting scientific-sounding phrases.

Ownership rule

AI may offer transformations, never substitute its question as the learner's. The interface might display:

  • “Make it more testable”
  • “Ask about the mechanism”
  • “Look for a boundary”

Only after the learner revises does the question enter the Trail as theirs. AI-generated candidate questions are visibly labelled and stored separately if retained.

15.2 Curiosity architecture

Curiosity is not a personality trait to maximize continuously. It is a state produced by a meaningful information gap that the learner believes can be reduced through action. Forge uses five reliable openings:

  1. prediction violation: an object behaves differently from the learner's model;
  2. contrast: two superficially similar cases produce different outcomes;
  3. hidden variable: an apparently irrelevant change matters;
  4. boundary: a familiar rule breaks under an extreme or altered assumption;
  5. consequence: the concept changes a real design, explanation or decision.

A good opening has three properties: the learner can understand the situation immediately, has a reason to predict, and can change something that matters. Mystery without agency becomes spectacle; agency without conceptual structure becomes random play.

Productive curiosity versus rabbit hole

Forge classifies a branch by its relationship to the current capability:

  • Core: answering it is necessary to complete the present model.
  • Bridge: not necessary, but it connects the model to another representation or application.
  • Frontier: worthwhile and learner-owned, but requires a later lab or project.
  • Drift: interesting but currently disconnected, unsafe, unanswerable with available evidence or likely to derail the learner's stated goal.

The interface never calls a question “drift.” It says, “That deserves its own trail. Save it for after we settle what changes the cart's velocity?” One branch may be opened immediately for up to three minutes when it illuminates the core. Others are preserved without turning the session into an infinite feed.

Curiosity must not become extraction

The product should not infer intimate interests to generate irresistible hooks. Interest memory is opt-in, editable and coarse: “likes skateboarding,” “interested in space,” “prefers building examples.” No psychographic advertising, emotional vulnerability targeting or algorithmic urgency is permitted. A lab can be useful without a personalized theme.

15.3 Intuition-building environment

An intuition is a compressed model that supports fast, roughly correct prediction—not a colourful analogy. Forge builds intuition through coordinated representations and controlled variation.

The microworld design grammar

Each lab should include:

  • one causal focus: no more than two new relations at once;
  • direct manipulation: learners change variables rather than only watch;
  • visible invariants: what remains constant is as clear as what changes;
  • multiple linked views: physical scene, force diagram, graph and equation update together;
  • prediction gate: run is disabled until the learner commits;
  • counterexample button: reveals a case that a common rule-of-thumb mishandles;
  • idealization control: friction, noise or constraints can be introduced after the clean model;
  • replay and compare: two trials can be overlaid;
  • explanation canvas: learner marks causal links rather than choosing only an answer;
  • export to reality: one observation or measurement relates the ideal world to ordinary experience.

Research on PhET emphasizes concrete real-world connections, representations not normally visible, analogies and dynamic feedback, but also the importance of productive constraints rather than feature abundance. Podolefsky, Perkins & Adams, 2010 Forge should borrow the interaction discipline, not assume that adding AI improves it.

Representation sequence

For the mechanics wedge:

  1. concrete animation and qualitative prediction;
  2. force or motion diagram;
  3. time-based graph;
  4. proportional reasoning;
  5. equation or code representation;
  6. physical observation with noise and friction.

The order can vary after diagnosis, but Forge should not ask a novice to coordinate every representation at once. A learner who succeeds in one view is later asked to translate rather than simply receive the translation.

Analogy policy

An analogy is useful only when the mapped relations and breakpoints are explicit. The system asks:

  • What corresponds to what?
  • Which relation is preserved?
  • Where does the analogy fail?

AI may generate candidate analogies, but only expert-reviewed analogy templates should be used in the MVP. A vivid but structurally wrong analogy is worse than a plain explanation.

Embodiment without theatrics

Physical actions should clarify the model, not decorate it. Examples include feeling a change in acceleration on a rolling chair under safe conditions, timing a toy car on tile and rug, balancing masses, filming a pendulum without faces, or tracing a graph while an animation runs. No motion is required where inaccessible; equivalent visual, haptic or data-based representations must exist.

15.4 Productive-struggle engine

Productive struggle is not time spent wrong. It is effort on a reachable problem with a plausible strategy, informative feedback and a reasonable expectation of progress. Forge must distinguish at least eight states.

State Observable evidence System response
Too easy Immediate correct transfer, high confidence, explanation contains causal relations Skip redundant exploration; offer boundary or harder contrast
Fluent but shallow Correct answer, weak explanation, fails representation switch Ask for mechanism or translation; do not reteach everything
Productive struggle Multiple informative attempts, explicit plan, narrowing error Wait; affirm strategy, not effort alone; permit another run
One-hint blockage Stable near-correct model with one missing distinction Give a cue or ask a discriminating question
Misconception competition Same coherent wrong prediction across contexts Use counterexample and contrast, then reconstruction
Prerequisite gap Fails a small diagnostic needed for the target relation Branch to compact repair and return

Signals

The engine can use:

  • accuracy and error type across attempts;
  • time since last meaningful action, interpreted cautiously;
  • simulation action sequence and whether variables are changed systematically;
  • whether the learner states a plan;
  • confidence before and after evidence;
  • explicit learner controls: I have a plan, give me a nudge, I am confused, change the view, I am tired, show the idea then let me rebuild it;
  • prerequisite probes;
  • prior evidence on the same capability.

It must not use camera-based emotion recognition, voice-stress inference or hidden personality scoring.

Hint ladder

Level Support Example Assistance weight
0 Wait / metacognitive probe “What variable are you trying to hold constant?” 0.25
1 Attention cue Highlight the force arrow that changed 0.5
2 Strategic hint “Compare the change in velocity, not the direction of motion.” 1.0

When uncertainty remains

The system should say, “I may be misreading what is blocking you. Let me test one possibility,” then offer a low-cost discriminating probe. It should not confidently label a misconception from a single answer. When two hypotheses remain plausible, it chooses the probe with the greatest expected information value and lowest frustration cost. If model confidence is low after two probes, it falls back to an authored neutral pathway or invites the learner to choose what feels confusing.

When to recommend a break

A break prompt requires either explicit learner indication or a pattern of low-information interaction that persists after one representation change. It should be neutral: “We have been circling without new evidence. Save the state and return, or see one clean explanation and rebuild it now?” No streak loss, scarcity message or guilt.

16. AI Dependency Governor

16.1 Goal: govern cognitive ownership, not minimize help

The wrong objective is “use as little AI as possible.” A blind learner using text-to-speech, a multilingual learner using translation or a novice receiving a timely worked example may become more independent through substantial assistance. The governor asks:

Did the system perform the cognitive operation the learner is trying to develop, and did later evidence show that the learner could perform it with less support?

16.2 The assistance contract

At the start of a lab, the interface names the protected operations in plain language:

  • “You will make the prediction.”
  • “You will decide what to change.”
  • “I can help you notice a pattern, but I will not explain it before you test your model.”
  • “At the end, the support panel will close for one new case.”

The learner can always request a full explanation. The consequence is pedagogical, not punitive: the current task becomes assisted evidence, and a reconstruction plus future proof is scheduled. The system never lies that help is unavailable.

16.3 Mandatory safeguards

  1. Attempt before assistance. A meaningful trace is required unless a diagnostic shows no relevant prerequisite.
  2. Hint ladder. Help proceeds from probe to cue to representation to example; the model cannot jump to the final answer because the learner asks repeatedly.
  3. Answer withholding by state, not prompt. Target answers live behind a policy service; the language model is not given them in states where it must not disclose them.
  4. Reconstruction after assistance. The learner closes the explanation, waits briefly, then recreates the mechanism from memory.
  5. Error identification. Learners compare their former model with a plausible alternative and name the decisive flaw.
  6. Model comparison. When AI provides a solution or explanation, the learner must test it against the world or a validator.
  7. Tool-free transfer. At least one immediate and one delayed task hide generative help.
  8. Fading. Matched challenges permit fewer or later supports after evidence improves.
  9. Source verification. Factual claims outside deterministic models are grounded in identified, vetted sources; uncertainty is visible.
  10. Periodic capability audit. The system tests a sample of mastered capabilities without assistance, rather than relying on activity history.

16.4 Three measures, not one vanity ratio

Assistance Load (AL)

AL = Σ (support_weight × target_operation_overlap × timing_multiplier)
  • support weight follows the hint ladder;
  • target-operation overlap ranges from 0 for accessibility-only support to 1 when the support performs the protected operation;
  • timing multiplier is higher when help arrives before a meaningful attempt and lower after multiple diagnostic attempts.

AL is reported per capability and normalized by task difficulty. It is not a moral score.

Assisted–Unaided Transfer Gap (AUTG)

AUTG = standardized assisted performance − standardized delayed unseen closed-tool performance

A large positive gap is a warning: the product makes the learner look better than they are independently. A small gap can reflect genuine learning or tasks that are too easy, so it must be interpreted with difficulty and baseline data.

Fading Velocity (FV)

FV = − slope of Assistance Load across matched-difficulty successful attempts

Positive FV means less support is required over time. Difficulty matching is essential; otherwise the product could “improve” the metric by selecting easier tasks.

Optional headline: Assistance-to-Independent-Evidence Ratio

AIER = weighted assistance units / independently successful transfer units

This ratio is useful for product monitoring but too reductive as a learner-facing score. A learner with a difficult prerequisite repair may need high assistance and still make excellent progress.

16.5 Dependency warning patterns

The governor flags review—not punishment—when it observes:

  • increasing help requests on matched or easier tasks;
  • correct assisted performance paired with repeated delayed failure;
  • immediate requests before reading or attempting;
  • inability to explain a generated answer;
  • high confidence based on AI agreement rather than evidence;
  • repeated avoidance of closed-tool proof;
  • transfer success only when surface features closely match the lab;
  • excessive conversational interaction with little manipulation or learner output.

The learner sees a specific recommendation: “The simulator is doing useful work, but the principle is not yet retrievable without it. Try a two-minute force-arrow reconstruction tomorrow.”

16.6 The AI must also be governable

The system logs every support action with:

  • state and allowed action set;
  • evidence used;
  • model recommendation;
  • policy decision;
  • content shown;
  • validator result;
  • whether the learner later transferred.

This permits offline evaluation of whether a hint policy actually improves learning. Foundation-model output must be versioned, reproducible where possible and replaceable. A provider change should not silently change the product's pedagogy.

17. North-Star and Guardrail Metrics

17.1 Critique of “Unregretted Learning Time”

Unregretted Learning Time is a strong ethical design principle because it rejects screen-time optimization and asks whether the learner later values the experience. It is insufficient as the sole north star for four reasons:

  1. retrospective judgment can reflect entertainment, identity and social desirability rather than learning;
  2. difficult practice may feel regrettable today and valuable later;
  3. young learners may be unable to estimate opportunity cost;
  4. a product could maximize self-reported value through persuasive design without improving capability.

Keep it as a delayed qualitative outcome and product ethic. Pair it with direct evidence of independent learning.

17.2 North-star metric

Delayed Unaided Transfer Gain per Active Learning Hour (DUTG/ALH)

DUTG/ALH = (7–14 day unseen closed-tool transfer score − calibrated baseline expectation) / active learning hours

Operational definition:

  • The transfer assessment uses pre-registered, expert-reviewed items not shown during learning.
  • The learner has no generative AI, hints or solution retrieval; accessibility supports remain.
  • Score is placed on an item-response or standardized scale that accounts for difficulty.
  • Baseline expectation comes from a randomized comparison or a validated pre-test model—not the learner's raw pre-score alone.
  • Active learning time excludes loading, idle time, navigation and optional open exploration.
  • Report the metric at cohort/product level with confidence intervals, not as a precise personal productivity ranking.

Why this metric: it rewards learning that persists, crosses context and uses time well. It penalizes a product that creates impressive supported performance without later capability.

17.6 Measurement architecture

A credible learning product needs an evaluation layer separate from the engagement analytics layer.

  • Product events: starts, exits, latency, crashes and interaction affordances.
  • Learning events: prediction, misconception probe, hint level, representation, reconstruction, transfer, delay and confidence.
  • Outcome events: validated assessment scores, artifact quality, question quality and voluntary continuation.
  • Safety events: harmful content, privacy request, boundary violation, scientific-content error and emotional-dependency signal.

Learning and safety analyses require pre-specified definitions, versioned tasks, model/provider version and content version. Product teams should not silently redefine mastery after results arrive.

18. User Experience and Screen Map

18.1 First-launch experience

Forge opens with no dashboard. A full-screen phenomenon shows two identical carts. One receives a short push; the other receives a weaker push for longer. Before either moves, the learner sees:

“Which cart will be moving faster after the pushes stop? You do not need a formula. Make your best prediction.”

Below are three choices plus draw it and talk it out. A compact note says, “Forge helps by testing your model, not by giving the answer first.” The learner can choose Physics class, I am curious, or I have a problem I cannot solve. No account is required until the first lab needs a delayed proof; a local trial should work immediately.

The learner is not asked to choose a learning style, career, avatar, grade-point goal or thirty-topic curriculum.

18.2 The first five minutes

0:00–0:40 — Predict. The learner chooses and states confidence. The result is not immediately shown if the explanation reveals ambiguity; one discriminating question appears.

0:40–1:40 — Manipulate. The learner controls push magnitude, duration and mass. Each run requires a quick direction/ranking prediction.

1:40–2:30 — Surprise. The overlaid velocity traces contradict the learner's initial rule. The system asks which variable better predicts the change.

2:30–3:30 — Minimal support. A force–time area representation appears only if needed. AI speaks one short cue; the learner moves a slider or sketches a relation.

3:30–4:30 — Reconstruction. The learner explains, “A stronger force for shorter can have the same impulse…” or, in the simpler force-motion lab, “When the push stops, the force stops but motion does not have to.”

4:30–5:00 — Novel test. A spacecraft case removes friction and familiar classroom imagery. The support panel closes.

The first aha must occur before signup friction or a long diagnostic. The complete proof can take longer.

18.3 Milestone experiences

First successful learning session

The session lasts 12–18 active minutes. It ends with a concise Evidence Card:

  • “You changed your model of force and motion.”
  • “You predicted a spacecraft case without help.”
  • “One graph translation remains open.”
  • “Two-minute return proof scheduled for next week.”
  • “Question saved: Can direction change without speed changing?”

No confetti. A subtle animation may show the initial and revised models side by side.

First “I understand this now” moment

The interface replays the learner's original prediction beside the new explanation and the decisive simulation result. The sense of progress comes from visible model change, not a generic praise statement.

First curiosity branch

After the learner asks about circular motion, Forge offers:

  • Open now for three minutes: manipulate direction-only acceleration;
  • Save as next lab: why orbiting objects keep falling;
  • Keep exploring current idea: return to graph translation.

The learner chooses. The system does not autoplay the next lesson.

First project or experiment

The learner chooses a no-special-equipment mission: roll the same object across two surfaces, mark equal time intervals from a slow-motion video, and compare how the velocity change relates to friction. They may enter observations manually; video upload is optional and processed ephemerally.

First return session

A two-minute cold-open asks a surface-different question before showing any previous notes. If successful, Forge says, “Still yours.” If not, it shows the original model conflict and offers a compact repair—not a shame-inducing lost streak.

First unaided mastery demonstration

The learner receives a diagram, graph and verbal scenario unlike the lab. They must predict, justify and identify one tempting wrong explanation. The result is a capability statement with evidence conditions, not a permanent certificate.

19. Detailed Example Session

Concept: motion does not require a continuing net force

Learner: Maya, 14, has completed a unit on Newton's laws. She can write F = ma and solve familiar substitution problems. On a worksheet she says a hockey puck moving right must have a rightward force because “otherwise it would stop.” She is mildly frustrated and has used a chatbot before, which explained inertia; the explanation felt clear, but the same mistake returned.

19.1 Encounter

The screen shows an overhead view of a puck on an air table. A hand gives it a brief push to the right and disappears. Three force diagrams appear for the moment after the hand loses contact:

  • A: no horizontal force;
  • B: a rightward force;
  • C: a smaller rightward force plus a leftward force.

Forge: “The puck is still moving right. Which diagram describes the horizontal forces after the hand is gone?”

Maya selects B, confidence 80%, and says, “It needs a force to keep going.” Forge stores the committed model and does not mark the answer wrong.

19.2 Diagnostic move

Forge: “One quick distinction: if the rightward force became twice as large, what would change first—its position, its velocity, or the way its velocity changes?”

Maya selects velocity. This does not distinguish all hypotheses, so the policy asks a second probe using equal-time motion dots. Maya says closer dots mean “less force,” revealing that she partly treats force as velocity rather than acceleration.

Forge displays a small uncertainty note only in the internal trace: likely force–velocity conflation, confidence 0.68; friction assumption also plausible.

19.3 First attempt in the microworld

The lab has three sliders: initial speed, horizontal force and friction. Friction begins at zero. The force control automatically returns to zero after a one-second push.

Before Run, Maya must predict the motion-dot pattern after the push stops. She chooses “dots get closer until it stops.”

The puck receives the push. The velocity arrows grow during the push and then stay equal after it. The force diagram shows no horizontal arrow after contact. The puck continues.

Maya drags friction upward without making the required prediction. Forge disables Run and asks, “What result are you trying to create?”

Maya: “I want it to slow down like real things.”

This is an informative plan, not random clicking.

19.4 Contrast and first hint

She predicts that with friction the puck slows. It does. She says, “So there was friction before too, just not shown.”

Forge does not contradict her with a lecture. It opens a two-trial compare view:

  • Trial 1: friction = 0; velocity constant after push;
  • Trial 2: friction > 0; leftward net force; velocity decreases.

Forge, Level 1 attention cue: “Watch the change in the velocity arrows when the net force is zero, then when it points left.”

Maya replays. “Oh. With no arrow, the speed doesn't change. The force is what changes it, not what makes it exist.”

19.5 Reconstruction

The Mechanism Notebook shows her initial sentence on the left: “It needs a force to keep going.” On the right is a blank relation:

net force → ________

Maya types “movement.” The concept checker does not accept it because it remains ambiguous.

Forge: “Your trials distinguish motion from change in motion. Which one followed the net force?”

Maya changes it to “change in velocity.”

She then draws:

  • zero net force → constant velocity;
  • leftward net force while moving right → velocity gets smaller;
  • rightward net force → rightward velocity changes.

Forge highlights one omission: direction can also change. It does not supply circular motion yet.

19.6 First-principles explanation

Maya speaks:

“A push doesn't leave a force stored in the puck. While the hand pushes, the net force changes the velocity. When the hand leaves and there is no friction, there is no horizontal net force, so the velocity stays the same. Real pucks slow because friction gives a force opposite the motion.”

The evaluator checks four required relations: contact ceases, no stored push, net force relates to acceleration/change in velocity, friction explains slowing. All are present. It notes that “stays the same” includes direction and speed in this one-dimensional case.

19.7 Transfer challenge with AI absent

The support panel closes. A spacecraft in deep space fires its engine for ten seconds, then shuts it off. The craft is far from significant gravity in the idealized model.

Question 1: draw the force arrow after shutdown.

Question 2: choose the velocity graph.

Question 3: explain why the wrong answer “velocity falls to zero because thrust is zero” is tempting.

Maya draws no force arrow but initially selects a velocity graph that slopes gently downward. The validator asks her to check consistency between her two answers; no hint is given. She pauses and changes the graph to a horizontal line.

Her explanation: “It is tempting because cars stop when you stop pressing, but that includes friction and air. The spacecraft case removes those.”

This counts as independent transfer with one self-corrected inconsistency and no cognitive assistance.

19.8 Reality mission

Forge offers three choices:

  1. toy car on smooth floor versus towel;
  2. roll a bottle and mark equal video intervals;
  3. analyze a pre-recorded accessible dataset.

Maya chooses the toy car. Before instructions, she writes a plan: “Use the same push on both.” Forge asks, “How will you make the push similar enough to compare?” She invents a small ramp and releases the car from the same mark. The product checks safety and suggests measuring distance travelled after leaving the ramp.

Her results are noisy: the car veers on the smooth surface and travels only slightly farther. She is disappointed.

Forge: “Does noisy evidence make the model wrong, or does it reveal another variable? Name two possibilities before I suggest any.”

Maya names wheel alignment and different contact with the towel. Forge helps her separate the ideal model from experimental control. The mission succeeds because she encounters measurement and confounding, not because the result looks textbook-perfect.

19.9 Learner-generated question

Maya asks, “If velocity is speed and direction, can something accelerate even if the speed stays exactly the same?”

Forge recognizes a connection question with strong causal potential. It offers an optional three-minute circular-motion branch. Maya opens it and rotates a velocity arrow around a circle while keeping its length constant. She predicts that no acceleration exists because the speed is constant, then notices the velocity difference vectors point inward.

Forge saves: “Next frontier—why a force perpendicular to motion changes direction but not speed.” It does not teach the full unit.

19.10 Delayed unaided demonstration

Eight days later, Maya opens Forge and receives a two-minute “Still yours?” task. A curling stone moves east while a constant northward force acts briefly. She must choose the direction of the velocity change, sketch the later velocity and explain what happens after the force stops.

No chat or prior notes are available. Maya correctly identifies the northward velocity change, draws the resultant velocity and says it remains constant after the force stops in the idealized case. She then identifies a wrong response that confuses final velocity with force direction.

Forge updates the evidence statement:

Retained across eight days: You can distinguish net force from velocity and transfer the model to a two-dimensional case without assistance. Circular-motion direction change is still emerging.

19.11 Why this is not an unrealistically perfect session

Maya makes three distinct errors, tries to manipulate the simulation without predicting, challenges an idealization, produces noisy physical data and nearly contradicts her force diagram with a sloped graph. The system does not interpret every struggle as beneficial. It supplies one cue, requires reconstruction, closes assistance for transfer and accepts that the real experiment raises complications. The learning claim rests on the later unfamiliar case, not on her saying “I get it.”

20.2 MVP stack

Frontend

  • Next.js/React TypeScript progressive web app. Web distribution avoids app-store delay and supports laptops, tablets and modern phones.
  • SVG/Canvas/WebGL only as needed. The first mechanics simulations can be deterministic SVG/Canvas. Do not introduce a game engine until it solves a real performance problem.

  • Accessible interaction layer. Keyboard control, screen-reader descriptions, reduced-motion mode, high contrast, scalable labels, non-drag alternatives and captioned audio.
  • Modular lab shell. Challenge, prediction, world, notebook and transfer surfaces share a common event protocol while each content lab supplies validated domain logic.

Backend

  • TypeScript/Node or Python FastAPI orchestration service. Choose the team's strongest language; separation between policy and content matters more than framework.
  • PostgreSQL for users, consent, capability evidence, concept graph, content versions and assessment scheduling.
  • Object storage for expert-authored assets and optional learner artifacts; use short retention for uploaded media.
  • Job queue for delayed proof scheduling, offline evaluation, model-quality checks and non-urgent artifact analysis.
  • Feature/version service that pins simulation, content, policy and model versions for every learning event.

A managed backend such as Supabase can reduce the first-week burden, provided row-level security, regional requirements and deletion workflows are configured correctly. The MVP does not require microservices, a vector database cluster, event streaming or a custom model.

20.5 Learner-state schema

A minimal relational model:

  • learner: pseudonymous ID, age band, language, accessibility preferences, consent state;
  • capability: domain, concept, operation, representation, standard version;
  • evidence_event: task, response hash or structured answer, correctness, confidence, timestamp, delay, content/model versions;
  • assistance_event: support type, weight, protected-operation overlap, policy state, reason;
  • misconception_hypothesis: candidate, probability band, supporting and contradicting event IDs, expiry;
  • question: learner text, AI transformations, ownership marker, stage, relation to capability;
  • artifact: type, provenance, rubric result, storage/retention class;
  • proof_schedule: due window, task family exclusions, completion state;
  • experiment_assignment: arm, consent, protocol version and exclusions.

Raw chat should not be the canonical learner state. Store structured learning evidence and the minimum excerpt needed to audit a score. This reduces privacy risk and prevents the learner model from becoming an uninspectable biography.

20.6 Model orchestration

Tasks suitable for a small, fast model

  • classify response form and language;
  • detect whether required causal terms are present;
  • summarize a learner's explanation without evaluating it;
  • map a support request to an allowed category;
  • translate approved content;
  • flag likely unsafe or off-topic input;
  • generate concise UI copy from a fixed template.

Tasks that may justify frontier reasoning

  • discriminate among two or three plausible misconception hypotheses using a full session trace;
  • interpret a rough multimodal sketch or spoken explanation;
  • propose a highly targeted contrast case under a strict validated template;
  • assess an open causal explanation against a detailed rubric while reporting uncertainty;
  • coach a learner-generated question without replacing ownership;
  • analyze an unusual physical mission result and separate measurement problems from conceptual errors.

Frontier calls should occur at pedagogical decision points, not every turn. Model output is structured JSON with confidence, evidence references and a proposed action. The policy service decides whether the action is allowed.

Model independence

Define an internal capability interface:

  • classify_evidence()
  • rank_hypotheses()
  • phrase_hint()
  • evaluate_explanation()
  • coach_question()
  • interpret_artifact()
  • verify_source_claim()

Each has a fixed schema, held-out evaluation set, latency budget and fallback. The application should support two providers for critical functions once the pilot begins; otherwise a provider outage or behavioural update changes the experiment.

20.7 Evaluation engine

Use the cheapest reliable evaluator in this order:

  1. exact state or numeric validator;
  2. symbolic algebra/unit validator;
  3. authored multiple-choice discriminator;
  4. rule-based concept rubric;
  5. small-model rubric scorer;
  6. frontier-model scorer with evidence and uncertainty;
  7. human review for research labels, safety disputes or low-confidence important decisions.

Open explanation scoring should use at least two signals—for example, a rule-based causal-relation check plus a model rubric. Disagreement does not become an automatic failure. It triggers a clarifying probe or marks the evidence uncertain.

For initial research, a stratified sample of explanations should be double-scored by humans to estimate false-positive and false-negative rates. A learner should be able to challenge a judgment in ordinary language.

20.8 Simulation and interactive-content architecture

Each lab is a versioned package containing:

  • concept and capability IDs;
  • state variables and valid ranges;
  • deterministic update function;
  • visual and accessible representations;
  • invariants and test suite;
  • prediction schema;
  • misconception probes;
  • allowed hint actions;
  • transfer templates;
  • reality missions;
  • scientific assumptions/idealizations;
  • content author and reviewer sign-off.

Generative coding can accelerate a draft but never publish directly. Required automated tests include conservation or law constraints where relevant, dimensional checks, edge cases, cross-browser rendering and equivalence between visual and accessible outputs. A physics educator must run an acceptance checklist.

The long-term system can generate new surface contexts around a stable causal kernel. It should not generate a new kernel in real time for children.

20.9 Source and retrieval layer

The first product needs a small trusted corpus: expert-authored concept notes, open educational resources, textbook excerpts where licensed, PhET research/design materials, safety protocols and a handful of primary or institutional sources for historical extensions. Every retrieved claim carries source identity, date and content version.

The retrieval layer should answer two distinct questions:

  • What explanation is pedagogically suitable now? Chosen by concept, prerequisite and support state.
  • What evidence supports this factual claim? Chosen by source quality and relevance.

A fluent model response with no source is never the ground truth for an externally factual question.

20.10 Safety architecture

  • Age-band policy and parental consent where legally required.
  • Input/output moderation independent of the tutor prompt.
  • No open web browsing by a minor-facing model in the MVP; extensions use an approved corpus.
  • Physical missions selected from a reviewed risk class; no chemicals, flames, roads, heights, weapons, electrical mains or unsupervised contact with strangers.
  • No precise location collection; local mission suggestions use learner-selected environment categories.
  • No human mentor contact in the MVP.
  • Clear “ask an adult/teacher” escalation for safety, abuse, self-harm, medical or legal disclosures, without pretending software is a confidential professional.
  • Scientific-content incident process that can disable a lab version immediately.

20.11 Privacy architecture

Privacy is a product function, not a policy document. The FTC's Edmodo action emphasized that an educational technology provider cannot shift COPPA responsibility to schools and cannot use school-authorized data for advertising; the FTC also finalized COPPA updates in 2025 strengthening data-use and retention protections. Forge should therefore implement:

  • no advertising or sale of learner data;
  • separate educational purpose limitation;
  • granular retention periods by data class;
  • learner and parent-readable memory view;
  • one-click export and deletion workflow;
  • cryptographic separation of account identity and research dataset;
  • no model training on identifiable learner content without separate, meaningful opt-in;
  • no third-party analytics SDK that creates advertising identifiers;

20.12 Observability and reliability

Monitor three planes separately:

Technical: p50/p95 latency, simulation frame rate, crash-free sessions, failed model calls, fallback rate, queue delay and data loss.

Pedagogical: state-transition distribution, hint escalation, diagnosis confidence, correction rate, delayed proof completion, AUTG and content-version outcome.

Safety: moderation categories, false-block appeals, privacy operations, physical-mission incidents, emotional-dependency language and scientific-content defects.

Every alert must identify a responsible owner. Model traces used for debugging should be sampled, redacted and time-limited.

21. Minimum Viable Product

21.1 The MVP thesis

The MVP exists to test one proposition:

A prediction-driven, deterministic microworld with governed assistance and delayed closed-tool transfer creates more independent conceptual capability than a high-quality explanation or ordinary AI help at comparable time.

Anything not required to test that proposition is excluded.

21.2 Must build

  1. Three to five mechanics labs with deterministic simulations:
    • velocity versus acceleration;
    • force versus motion;
    • force–mass–acceleration;
    • optional motion graphs;
    • optional friction/idealization.
  2. Prediction capture through multiple choice plus short text/drawing; voice can wait.
  3. A fixed misconception library with discriminating probes for the selected concepts.
  4. Policy-owned hint ladder with at least five support levels and complete assistance logging.
  5. Mechanism reconstruction with transparent, expert-authored causal rubrics.
  6. Immediate unseen transfer with chat and hints disabled.
  7. Seven-day proof scheduler and two-minute closed-tool return task.
  8. Basic capability evidence card showing what transferred, assistance used and what remains uncertain.
  9. One optional no-material reality bridge per lab.
  10. Research instrumentation for pre-test, time, actions, assistance, immediate outcome, delayed outcome and attrition.
  11. Privacy essentials: pseudonymous accounts, consent, data deletion, no ads, no raw media retention.
  12. Content/admin console for the internal team, not for schools: publish versions, inspect lab events and disable a faulty item.

22. Seven-Day Build Plan—and the Gates Beyond It

A seven-day sprint can prove the interaction, not the educational effect. The plan below separates demonstration from evidence.

0–24h

Prove the distinctive interaction

User-visible: one force-versus-motion anomaly, committed prediction, deterministic simulation, one hint level, reconstruction and closed transfer.

Technical: Next.js shell; local simulation; session state machine; event schema; one fixed validator; hard-coded content JSON.

Evaluation: five internal think-aloud runs. Record whether users can describe how it differs from chat and whether the central misconception is exposed.

Gate: stop polishing if the learner can complete the loop by random clicking or if the first discrepancy takes more than five minutes.

Days 2–3

Make assistance governance real

User-visible: two additional contrast cases; five-level hint ladder; “change view” control; initial-versus-revised model display.

Technical: policy service separate from model; small-model explanation classifier; assistance ledger; representation sync; authored fallback hints.

Evaluation: 8–12 students or near-age users, including learners with the target misconception. Check bypass attempts and false diagnoses.

Gate: the AI must be unable to disclose the target answer in protected states, even under prompt pressure.

Days 4–7

Complete the minimum Capability Loop

User-visible: three labs, immediate transfer, evidence card, saved question, account after value, seven-day return proof scheduling.

Technical: Postgres/Supabase, authentication, content versioning, experiment assignment, deletion workflow, basic observability and research export.

Evaluation: 15–25 learners complete one lab; compare immediate transfer and friction with a good authored explanation in a crossover or small randomized usability test.

Gate: at least 70% reach closed transfer, fewer than 15% report answer-withholding as the main reason for stopping, and no scientific or privacy blocker remains. These are product gates, not efficacy proof.

Weeks 2–4

Turn a demo into a research prototype

User-visible: five complete labs, fast diagnostic bypass, accessibility modes, optional reality bridges and first return proofs.

Technical: validated item pools; model evaluation harness; source retrieval; admin version controls; latency/cost routing; safety red-team suite.

Evaluation: 40–60 learners; blind human coding of explanations and questions; estimate transfer-task reliability and AUTG.

Gate: delayed proof completion and scoring reliability are adequate to justify an RCT; otherwise narrow the claim or redesign the loop.

Months 2–3

Run the first honest comparative pilot

User-visible: stable five-lab mechanics unit and transparent research consent.

Technical: preregistered analytics, assignment integrity, cohort reporting, two model-provider fallback for critical functions and audited privacy controls.

23. Five-Minute Demonstration

The demonstration must show the product mechanism, not a narrated feature tour.

Exact sequence

0:00–0:25 — The committed model

On screen: a puck receives a brief rightward push, then the hand disappears. The presenter asks the audience member or learner to choose the force diagram after release and state confidence.

The learner selects a rightward force because the puck moves right. The interface records the answer and displays no red mark.

Proof: Forge begins with the learner's model rather than a generated explanation.

0:25–0:55 — The manipulable world

The learner sets friction to zero, predicts “slows down,” and runs the simulation. Motion dots remain evenly spaced; the force arrow disappears while velocity remains.

They turn friction on and predict again. A leftward net-force arrow appears and the velocity arrows shrink.

Proof: Consequence comes from deterministic physics; the learner is changing causal variables.

0:55–1:25 — Intelligent restraint

The learner presses Help. Instead of explaining inertia, Forge asks, “Which trial changed the velocity arrows after the push—the one with zero net force or the one with a leftward net force?”

The learner answers. A small support indicator records attention cue, not “wrong.”

Proof: AI contributes the minimum useful move and assistance is visible.

1:25–2:05 — Model reconstruction

Forge shows the learner's original sentence: “Force keeps it moving.” The learner completes net force → change in ______ and explains the friction contrast aloud or in text.

The evaluator identifies one ambiguity and asks for direction or speed. The learner revises to “change in velocity.”

Proof: The learner reconstructs the causal relation; AI does not replace the explanation.

2:05–2:50 — Assistance disappears

A spacecraft fires thrusters, then shuts them off. Chat and hint controls visibly collapse. The learner draws no post-shutdown force and selects a constant-velocity graph. A contradictory answer would trigger a consistency check, not a hint.

Proof: The product measures independent transfer, not supported fluency.

2:50–3:35 — Curiosity becomes a question

The learner asks, “Can something accelerate if its speed does not change?” Forge labels it as a connection question and offers a miniature direction-only vector manipulation. The learner rotates a velocity arrow while keeping its length fixed and sees the change vector.

Proof: Curiosity grows from the solved mechanism and remains bounded to a productive next frontier.

3:35–4:15 — Knowledge touches reality

Forge offers a safe mission: compare the same toy car after leaving a short ramp on two surfaces, or use a supplied dataset. It asks the learner to plan how to keep the initial condition comparable before giving a protocol.

Proof: The system moves from ideal model to measurement and control rather than ending at screen correctness.

4:15–4:40 — Evidence, not engagement

The Evidence Card says:

  • initial model recorded;
  • attention cue used;
  • spacecraft transfer independent;
  • graph translation demonstrated;
  • physical mission optional;
  • seven-day proof pending.

No streak, level or session-time celebration appears.

Proof: The product's object is capability under conditions.

4:40–5:00 — The future test

The presenter opens a pre-seeded eight-day return proof: a curling stone with a brief perpendicular force. Forge hides all support. The learner predicts the velocity change and explains what persists after the force stops.

Closing line: “The product did not win because its AI answered better. It won only if this answer is now the learner's.”

24. Evaluation and Falsification Plan

24.1 Research question

Does the Forge Capability Loop produce greater delayed unaided conceptual transfer than ordinary chatbot assistance, a high-quality authored explanation with guided practice, or unaided conventional study at comparable active time?

24.2 Phase 0: measurement validation

Before an efficacy comparison:

  • develop three equivalent item families per capability;
  • obtain physics-expert review for content validity;
  • conduct cognitive interviews with 20 students;
  • estimate item difficulty and misconception discrimination in 80–150 responses where feasible;
  • double-score open explanations to establish rubric reliability;
  • test whether the closed transfer truly differs in surface and representation from practice;
  • verify that the assessment can detect known novice–expert differences without being merely a reading test.

The product cannot prove transfer with weak items.

24.3 Pilot design

Population

120–200 students aged 13–16 currently studying or recently exposed to introductory mechanics. Recruit across at least two schools or community programmes and include a range of prior attainment. Obtain assent and appropriate parental/institutional consent.

Four conditions

  1. Forge: full prediction, microworld, governed hints, reconstruction, transfer and delayed proof.
  2. Ordinary frontier chatbot: same learning objective and time budget; students can ask naturally, with standard safety rules but no Forge governor.
  3. Strong authored explanation: concise expert video/text, annotated examples, high-quality guided worksheet and immediate feedback; optionally a PhET simulation with an authored worksheet where appropriate.
  4. Conventional self-study: textbook/notes and practice resources normally available to the learner, with the same time window and no generative AI.

The strongest causal comparison is Forge versus condition 3, not versus no support. Condition 2 estimates the assisted–unaided risk in realistic AI use.

Assignment

Randomize within site and baseline capability band. Where learners complete multiple concept units, a partially crossed design can improve power, but carryover must be controlled: no student receives different conditions for closely related concepts in the same period. Analysts should be blinded to condition for open-response scoring.

Duration

  • baseline assessment;
  • two learning sessions of 15–20 active minutes over one week;
  • immediate transfer within the session;
  • seven-day unseen unaided assessment;
  • thirty-day retention follow-up if feasible;
  • optional curiosity continuation window of seven days after each session.

24.4 Outcomes

Primary outcome

Seven-day unseen, unaided conceptual-transfer score, standardized within validated item family and pre-registered before data inspection.

Secondary outcomes

  • immediate learning gain;
  • 30-day retention;
  • explanation quality and causal completeness;
  • representation translation;
  • question quality on a common novel phenomenon;
  • Assistance Load and AUTG;
  • calibration between confidence and correctness;
  • time on task and productive cognitive activity;
  • voluntary return to a saved question without push pressure;
  • learner experience, including effort, value, frustration and delayed regret;
  • attrition and bypass behaviour;
  • subgroup outcomes and diagnostic error.

Delayed Unregretted Learning Time item

At seven days, ask:

“Thinking back to the learning session, how much of that time now feels worth having spent—and why?”

Code reasons into capability, insight, curiosity, artifact, confidence, enjoyment, compliance or regret. Do not reduce this to one star rating.

24.5 Analysis

  • intention-to-treat is primary;
  • adjust for pre-registered baseline capability and site;
  • report effect sizes and confidence intervals, not only p-values;
  • correct or hierarchically model multiple secondary outcomes;
  • analyze attrition by arm and use sensitivity bounds for missing delayed tests;
  • distinguish time-normalized and raw outcomes;
  • test whether assistance mediates immediate–delayed differences, labelled exploratory unless pre-registered;
  • report all protocol deviations, model/content versions and incidents;
  • publish de-identified task definitions and analysis code where consent and licensing allow.

With roughly 40 learners per arm, the study is a feasibility and moderate-to-large-signal pilot, not a definitive small-effect trial. Under simple pairwise assumptions, 40 per arm is only well powered for effects around d ≈ 0.63; clustering, attrition and multiple comparisons raise the requirement. A confirmatory study should target roughly 100 or more per arm, adjusted using observed intraclass correlation and attrition.

24.6 Success thresholds

A reasonable “continue” gate after the pilot:

  • Forge exceeds the strong authored baseline by at least a practically meaningful pre-registered margin on seven-day transfer, with uncertainty compatible with a later confirmatory trial;
  • Forge's AUTG is materially smaller than the ordinary-chatbot condition;
  • at least 65% of starters reach closed transfer without coercive design;
  • scientific-content and safety defect rates remain acceptably low;
  • question-quality or curiosity benefits do not require sacrificing transfer;
  • no subgroup shows a material harm pattern that the design cannot plausibly repair.

The pilot should not be declared successful merely because Forge beats conventional self-study or receives high satisfaction.

24.7 Falsification criteria

The product thesis is weakened or falsified if any of the following persists after one targeted redesign:

  1. No transfer advantage: seven-day unaided performance is indistinguishable from, or worse than, the strong authored baseline at comparable time.
  2. Performance illusion: Forge greatly improves in-session success but AUTG resembles unrestricted chatbot assistance.
  3. Friction without benefit: answer withholding causes high abandonment and the completers do not gain more.
  4. Simulation-specific learning: learners succeed only on visually similar tasks and fail textual, graphical or physical representations.
  5. Diagnosis adds no value: a fixed, well-designed hint sequence performs as well as model-mediated diagnosis.
  6. Authoring economics fail: high-quality labs require so much expert time that the narrow product cannot reach a sustainable catalogue.
  7. Advantage is selection: benefits occur only among already motivated or high-attaining learners and disappear under intention-to-treat analysis.
  8. Dependency worsens: required assistance does not fade or learners become less willing to attempt before asking.
  9. Safety or privacy cost is unacceptable: minor-facing AI cannot be operated without surveillance, data overcollection or frequent harmful errors.

24.8 What would justify scaling

Scaling from five labs to a full mechanics course requires replicated gains across at least three concept families, stable content-production time, retained outcomes at 30 days and evidence that the loop works outside the founding team's direct facilitation. Expanding to another subject requires a new theory of domain representation and new transfer validation; physics success does not authorize a generic “learn anything” claim.

25. Safety and Privacy

25.1 Developmental boundary

The first public product should be designed for ages 13–16, with local legal review and age assurance proportionate to risk. It should not quietly accept younger users under the same design. A later 9–12 version would require shorter sessions, more parent/teacher mediation, reduced open generation, different language and consent, and more stringent content and memory limits.

Forge is a learning tool, not a therapist, friend, school authority, emergency service or confidential adviser. It should say so in behaviour, not only in terms of service.

UNICEF's 2025 Guidance on AI and Children places safety, privacy, non-discrimination, transparency, accountability, development, inclusion and child participation among the requirements for child-centred AI. 25.4 Model memory

Memory should be capability-scoped and inspectable. The learner can see entries such as:

  • “You previously confused force with velocity in one-dimensional motion.”
  • “You asked to revisit circular motion.”
  • “Text plus diagram is currently available; graph translation remains unproven.”

It should not contain hidden statements such as “anxious learner,” “low persistence,” political identity, family conflict or inferred vulnerability. Misconception hypotheses expire when contradicted. Learners can mark an inference wrong and request deletion.

25.5 Emotional dependency and persuasive design

Forge must not:

  • claim consciousness, affection or personal need;
  • encourage secrecy from parents, teachers or friends;
  • position itself as the learner's best or only source of understanding;
  • use guilt, loss, streak repair, countdowns or social comparison to induce return;
  • send late-night or high-frequency notifications;
  • personalize praise to exploit insecurity;
  • prolong conversation after the learning objective is complete;
  • reward disclosure unrelated to the task;
  • simulate human mentorship where no human is involved.

A learner may appreciate the tool and its voice. The system should redirect relational language toward the learner's own work: “You did the hard part—your prediction and revision created the insight.”

25.6 Misinformation and epistemic safety

  • Core world claims are executable or source-linked.
  • The UI distinguishes model, idealization, observation and external factual claim.
  • Generated explanation contains a “why this is trustworthy” affordance showing deterministic rule or source.
  • The system states when a question is contested, outside scope or not answerable from present evidence.
  • Learners practise finding an AI error; the product periodically presents a flawed generated explanation for audit.
  • Source quality and date matter; links are not decorative.
  • The learner can report a scientific issue without losing progress.

25.7 Bias and evaluation error

Open language may be scored differently across dialects, languages and disability-related communication styles. Therefore:

  • evaluate causal content separately from grammar and polish;
  • offer drawing, speech, equation and manipulation evidence;
  • calibrate scorers on multilingual and accessibility samples;
  • show the rubric in learner-friendly form;
  • use “uncertain—ask one clarification” rather than forcing a score;
  • never use Forge evidence alone for grades, tracking, discipline or admission;
  • provide a correction process and retain the original/changed evidence provenance.

25.8 Accessibility

Accessibility is not an add-on after efficacy. The first labs need:

  • full keyboard and switch-compatible controls;
  • non-drag equivalents for every slider/action;
  • semantic descriptions of diagrams and animated state;
  • sonified or tabular alternatives to visual graphs where useful;
  • captions and transcripts;
  • adjustable pace without time penalty;
  • reduced motion and high contrast;
  • language simplification without lowering conceptual standard;
  • low-bandwidth and offline-capable task packages;
  • no requirement for handwriting, speech, camera or fine motor control;
  • accessible physical-mission alternatives.

25.9 Parent and teacher roles

For 13–16-year-olds, parents should receive a plain explanation of data, boundaries and the learning method—not a live surveillance feed. With learner knowledge, a parent or teacher can see an evidence summary:

  • capability attempted;
  • unaided transfer evidence;
  • type of support used;
  • open question;
  • suggested human conversation or activity.

They should not receive raw chats by default, inferred emotions, every error or comparative rank. Teachers may assign an optional Forge lab later, but the learner must know exactly what is shared.

25.10 Autonomy versus protection

The learner should be able to:

  • stop a session without penalty;
  • request the full explanation;
  • decline a physical mission;
  • turn off interest memory;
  • see and correct inferred evidence;
  • export questions and artifacts;
  • delete the account;
  • choose whether a parent/teacher sees a specific evidence card where law and school context permit.

Protection is stronger when it preserves agency. Intrusive monitoring may reduce honest prediction and disclosure, damage trust and create a richer harm surface.

26.1 The natural expansion path

26.2 Year one: prove repeatability

The first year should not chase every STEM subject. The objective is a stable mechanics catalogue where:

  • labs share a reusable content schema;
  • delayed transfer gains replicate across concept families;
  • the dependency governor improves over a fixed hint script;
  • content authoring time declines without quality loss;
  • accessibility and low-bandwidth modes work;
  • teachers trust the evidence summary even when they did not assign the lab.

A possible consumer product can charge families modestly for a full catalogue while maintaining a meaningful free tier and research/school partnerships. Pricing strategy must not make delayed proof or safe AI behaviour premium-only.

26.3 Three years: a personal laboratory

Expansion should follow domains where a learner can manipulate an explicit model and independent transfer is measurable:

  1. algebraic functions and transformations;
  2. probability and statistical intuition;
  3. circuits and energy systems;
  4. chemical equilibrium and molecular interaction where validated representations exist;
  5. introductory algorithms, program state and debugging;
  6. simple economics and systems models with assumptions visible.

Each domain needs its own misconception research, representation grammar, validators and human reviewers. “One model can tutor every subject” is not a content strategy.

At this stage, Forge can let approved educators and institutions author labs using constrained templates. Publication requires automated tests, domain review and transfer-task quality checks. The authoring platform becomes a strategic asset only after the internal team proves the grammar.

26.4 Five years: curiosity becomes coordinated work

The learner's Trail matures from a saved question into a portable graph linking:

  • questions;
  • capabilities;
  • sources and evidence;
  • models and simulations;
  • experiments and artifacts;
  • people who provided critique;
  • unresolved contradictions;
  • delayed proof history.

A Project Compiler can transform one learner-owned ambition into capability requirements and selected Forge labs. Mentor Mesh can prepare short human interactions using the evidence trail. Apprentice Commons can unlock bounded real tasks only when prerequisite evidence and safeguarding are adequate.

The long-term object is not a personalized content playlist. It is an intellectual workspace where curiosity can become disciplined capability and responsible action.

26.5 Ten years: an alternative learning institution, not a replacement childhood

If evidence and trust justify it, Forge can support community laboratories, libraries, schools, museums, youth organizations and apprenticeships. A learner might use a local workshop to build a sensor, a remote scientist to critique a question, a school to supply shared history and civic knowledge, and Forge to coordinate models and evidence.

It should not attempt to replace school childcare, friendship, sport, art, civic community, safeguarding or human mentorship. An AI-native institution is valuable only when it orchestrates richer human and physical experiences rather than drawing more childhood into software.

26.6 What compounds

1. Validated concept and representation graph

The graph records not only prerequisites but which contrast, representation and transfer families reveal a capability. Domain depth and validation are harder to copy than topic labels.

2. Misconception-to-intervention evidence

Over time, Forge can learn which probe best discriminates among hypotheses and which support move improves delayed transfer for which evidence state. This is valuable only if gathered ethically and evaluated causally rather than as correlation-heavy “personalization.”

3. Transfer and delayed-retention bank

A large, calibrated pool of unseen tasks with known difficulty, contamination exclusions and cross-representation properties is a core moat. Generic models can generate questions; they cannot instantly produce trusted longitudinal measurement.

4. Pedagogical policy

The state machine, assistance taxonomy, dependency measures and audited intervention rules form an operational science of fading. Prompts alone are not defensible; measured policies and evaluation harnesses can be.

5. Deterministic microworld library

Validated executable models, accessible representations, idealization controls and reality bridges require sustained expert and engineering work.

6. Learner-owned evidence and question graph

With permission and portability, a longitudinal graph makes Forge more useful because the learner does not need to explain every prior model. The data must remain exportable; lock-in is not a legitimate moat.

7. Author and mentor network

Trusted educators, scientists, museums, makerspaces and mentors can expand reach and legitimacy. Network value comes from reviewed contributions and relationships, not a marketplace of unverified generated courses.

8. Evidence credibility

Pre-registration, negative-result publication, versioned content and independent replication can become a reputational asset. Many learning products claim efficacy; fewer build a product around being falsifiable.

26.7 What does not constitute defensibility

  • access to the same frontier model as competitors;
  • secret prompts;
  • a mascot or tutor personality;
  • generic conversation history;
  • a large quantity of generated lessons;
  • engagement streaks;
  • proprietary opacity in how children are scored;
  • school contracts alone;
  • collecting more child data than others;
  • a claim that an AI “knows the learner.”

26.8 Platform risk

Model providers can copy Study Mode, simulations and hinting. Forge's response is not to hide. It is to own the capability protocol, content validity, transfer evidence and learner trust. Where appropriate, Forge should publish open standards for assistance disclosure and portable capability evidence. A standard can expand the ecosystem while the product competes on execution, content and research quality.

27. Strongest Reasons Not to Build It

The case for Forge is strong enough to test, not strong enough to believe without evidence.

27.1 The best active baseline may already be sufficient

A well-designed PhET simulation plus an expert worksheet, retrieval schedule and teacher discussion may match Forge without expensive AI. If so, the model layer adds risk and cost rather than value. The first trial must include this baseline.

27.2 Students may not voluntarily choose cognitive effort

The target moment—confusion before an exam—often creates demand for immediate completion, not capability. A product that withholds answers can lose to a second browser tab. Forge may require school, parent or teacher endorsement to sustain use, weakening the direct-to-learner thesis.

27.3 Delayed learning is a difficult consumer value proposition

The benefit appears after time and is hard to feel at purchase. The learner may value the aha but ignore the future proof. A business built on a scientifically honest outcome can be commercially weaker than one built on answers, anxiety and credentials.

27.4 High-quality microworlds are expensive

Each concept requires scientific, pedagogical, interaction, accessibility and assessment work. Generated code reduces implementation time but not conceptual validation. The content business may scale too slowly for venture economics.

27.5 Diagnosis may be less useful than careful fixed design

A small set of common misconceptions and a well-authored sequence could outperform probabilistic model diagnosis. The system may feel personalized while adding little. If a fixed hint policy performs equally, Forge should simplify and reduce AI.

27.6 Transfer is hard to measure without teaching the test

A company that owns both instruction and assessment can unintentionally optimize content to its own transfer bank. Item leakage, common templates and scorer drift can inflate evidence. Independent researchers and external tasks are necessary, raising cost and slowing iteration.

27.7 Physics may be an unusually favourable domain

Causal models are executable, answers are often objective and misconceptions are well studied. Success may not generalize to history, writing, ethics, design or interpersonal judgment. The larger “learning operating system” vision could be an illusion built on a special case.

27.8 The product may narrow wonder into measurement

By requiring every curiosity branch to connect to a capability, Forge can turn play into instrumented work. Childhood interests sometimes deserve wandering, private obsession and uselessness. The product must leave space—and sometimes close itself.

27.9 Capability data can become a high-stakes shadow record

Even a nuanced evidence graph may be demanded by schools, employers or parents for selection and monitoring. A tool designed to support growth could become an accountability system that freezes early evidence into identity.

27.10 Minor-facing AI carries nontrivial risk

Misinformation, emotional dependence, data breaches, bias, unsafe missions and regulatory complexity can overwhelm a small team. The safest product might be an educator tool or an 18+ product first.

27.11 Incumbents can bundle the core feature

Khan Academy, Brilliant, PhET, general AI platforms and textbook publishers have content or distribution advantages. Once Forge demonstrates value, they can add prediction gates and delayed quizzes. The independent company needs a faster research/content loop and stronger trust than a feature copy.

27.12 The opportunity cost may be higher elsewhere

AI support for human tutors, teacher planning, special education access, literacy intervention or administrative burden may produce larger near-term social returns. Tutor CoPilot suggests one route where AI augments scarce human expertise at low cost. Stanford SCALE, 2024–25 A team motivated primarily by measured impact rather than a direct-to-student product should compare these opportunities explicitly.

28. Final Build Recommendation

The decision

Build recommendation

Build Forge now—but build only the Capability Loop, only for a small mechanics wedge, and only as a falsifiable research product.

Do not begin by building an AI tutor. Begin with one deterministic phenomenon that exposes a common model, one governed support ladder, one unfamiliar closed transfer task and one delayed return proof.

What to build in the next seven days

  1. A puck-and-friction microworld.
  2. A prediction canvas that stores the learner's pre-help model.
  3. A hard state machine that prevents answer leakage.
  4. One diagnostic probe for force–velocity conflation.
  5. A five-level hint ladder with assistance accounting.
  6. A learner reconstruction canvas.
  7. An unseen spacecraft transfer task with no AI.
  8. A seven-day curling-stone proof task.
  9. A capability evidence card.
  10. Instrumentation sufficient to compare against an expert explanation.

This is a small product, but it demonstrates the whole invention.

The 90-day wager

By day 90, the team should know whether:

  • students willingly enter the prediction–consequence loop;
  • the governor provides more useful support than a fixed script;
  • the learner can transfer after seven days;
  • assistance fades;
  • the content can be produced at a sustainable rate;
  • adults understand and trust the evidence;
  • the experience creates genuine curiosity without engagement manipulation.

If those signals are absent, stop or narrow. Do not cover more subjects, add a mascot or optimize notifications to disguise a failed learning mechanism.

Final product thesis in one line

Forge makes AI valuable by deciding when it must disappear.

Its deepest contribution is not personalized explanation. It is a reliable transition from a learner's current model, through evidence and bounded help, to a capability that survives the absence of the machine.

Research limitations

  1. The attached report is a synthesis, not a product trial. Its strongest value is constraining design; it cannot validate Forge.
  2. Component evidence does not guarantee package effects. Retrieval, guidance, simulation, tutoring and autonomy can interact badly when implemented together.
  3. Much AI-in-education research is recent, short-term and higher-education-heavy. Product announcements and company A/B tests often measure near-term behaviour rather than delayed transfer.
  4. Publication and survivorship bias affect both ed-tech histories and biographies. Failed internal pilots and ordinary learners leave fewer records than famous successes.
  5. Product outcomes depend on implementation. The label “AI tutor,” “simulation” or “project-based learning” predicts less than content quality, support policy, teacher context and learner population.
  6. The decision matrix is judgmental. It improves comparison but does not convert uncertain strategy into objective fact.
  7. The future-capability map is scenario analysis. AI trajectories, labour institutions, regulation and access can change substantially by 2035.
  8. Legal requirements vary. COPPA, Canadian provincial privacy rules, UK Age Appropriate Design, EU GDPR/AI rules and school contracting require jurisdiction-specific counsel.
  9. A consumer product may select motivated families. Efficacy must be tested in ordinary settings and with students who did not self-select for curiosity.
  10. No evidence yet establishes sustainable economics. Authoring cost, customer acquisition, willingness to pay and institutional distribution remain open.

Annotated critical source set

Bastani et al. (2025), PNAS — generative AI and high-school mathematics
The most decision-relevant warning: unrestricted GPT improved practice performance while harming later unaided performance; guardrails mitigated the harm.
Four sessions, one domain and product configuration; not a universal estimate of AI effects.
OECD Digital Education Outlook 2026
Frames the distinction between tool-supported performance and learning, and reviews governance, pedagogy, equity and system implications.
Synthesis and policy analysis; evidence base is evolving rapidly.
Agarwal, Nunes & Blunt (2021) — retrieval practice in schools
Supports repeated closed reconstruction and notes that classroom evidence generally favours retrieval.
The reviewed evidence is geographically narrow and varies in task and implementation.
Cepeda et al. (2006) — distributed practice review
Foundational evidence for delayed proof and spacing rather than one-session mastery.
Many tasks are simpler than conceptual transfer; optimal intervals depend on retention horizon.
Sinha & Kapur (2021) — productive failure meta-analysis
Supports carefully designed pre-instruction generation followed by consolidation, with boundary conditions.
Fidelity and age/domain matter; it does not justify unguided struggle.
Lazonder & Harmsen (2016) — guidance in inquiry learning
Supports constrained exploration rather than a binary between direct instruction and discovery.
Forms and quality of guidance vary; product translation requires care.
Podolefsky, Perkins & Adams (2010) — PhET design and learning
Provides the strongest practical design lineage for manipulable worlds, linked representations and engaged exploration.
Simulation effects depend on task design and surrounding instruction; not an AI study.
Kulik & Fletcher (2016) — intelligent tutoring systems meta-analysis
Shows that bounded, domain-specific diagnosis and feedback can improve learning.
Effects vary and can be larger on local measures; does not establish broad transfer.
Nickow, Oreopoulos & Quan (2020) — tutoring meta-analysis
Supports contingent, intensive feedback as a high-impact educational mechanism and sets a strong human benchmark.
Human tutoring conditions differ from software; scale and cost vary.
Tutor CoPilot randomized evaluation
Demonstrates a promising alternative design: AI can augment human tutor practice, with larger gains for less experienced tutors.
Requires an existing tutoring programme and measures a specific context.
Cristia et al. (2017) — One Laptop per Child in Peru
A canonical warning that access and use do not automatically create academic learning.
Specific rural context, hardware and implementation period; does not show devices are never valuable.
RAND (2017) — personalized learning
Shows modest positive signals alongside teacher workload, implementation ambiguity and material-quality problems.
Nonrandomized school comparisons and heterogeneous models.
OpenAI Study Mode documentation
Documents the current state of broad conversational study support and acknowledges direct-answer inconsistency and errors.
Product documentation, not independent efficacy evidence.
Khan Academy (2026) — Khanmigo product experiments
Shows serious large-scale iteration on prerequisites, context and cognitive engagement, and provides a high-quality incumbent comparison.
Company-reported near-term product metrics; delayed far transfer remains unestablished.
FTC Edmodo action (2023)
Makes educational-purpose limitation, operator responsibility, minimization and advertising boundaries concrete.
United States legal context; not legal advice for other jurisdictions.
UNICEF Guidance on AI and Children v3 (2025)
Provides a child-rights framework covering safety, privacy, fairness, transparency, development and inclusion.
Policy guidance rather than product-effect evidence; needs local implementation.
ILO (2025) — generative AI occupational exposure
Supports a future model centred on task transformation, judgment and human complementarity rather than simple job disappearance.
Exposure is not realized automation; technology and institutions will change.
Huntington Library — Octavia E. Butler papers
Primary archival evidence for notebooks, research materials, drafts and sustained revision among an exceptional learner/creator.
A single extraordinary life; not a causal recipe for ordinary learners.

Final operating philosophy

An AI-native learning system should increase the learner's contact with reality, evidence, difficult thought, useful knowledge and other people—not merely increase contact with AI. It should provide intelligence at the exact moment intelligence expands the learner's agency, and withdraw it at the exact moment assistance would take ownership away.

The smallest defensible version of that philosophy is Forge.
